Testing and Improving Your Interactive Project

In this activity, you will investigate and improve the user experience.
  1. Reflect on your development process. Reread your original project plans. What changes did you make as you built it and why?
  2. Gather user feedback.
    1. Share your project with 2-3 other people, and watch them use it without giving them any hints.

      2. Why can't I give the user hints about how to use my project?

      You probably haven't met the people who developed most of the software you use. Offering users technical support is expensive. So developers try to make apps that users can figure out on their own. Watching other people use your program without your help will help you understand how people might use (or struggle to use) your program and how you might improve it.

      How do I share a Snap! project?

      Choose "Open" from the Snap! File menu (), select this project from the list, press "Share," and click "Cancel" to close the box. Then copy and share the URL (web address) out of the location bar if your browser window.

    2. When they are finished reviewing your project, ask the users about their experiences and how your app could be improved. Here are some prompts to discuss:
      • Were you able to figure out how to use the project without help?
      • Describe any difficulties you faced.
      • Do you have any suggestions for improvement?
  3. Describe your reflections from observing other people use your project.
    • What did you observe the users do or say?
    • Did they do anything unexpected?
    • What did you learn watching them use your project?

    For example, for a sports game project, you might write: "I saw the user get frustrated by how quickly the soccer ball faded away. I learned that my game is harder to play on a bigger screen. If I had time, I'd let the user adjust how quickly the ball fades. I want to have the game suggest soccer movies, but I don't know how to find or search through movie data yet. Today, I'll add more costumes so the user can play with basketballs or baseballs."

  4. You don't have to do everything the users suggest. (Programmers often don't have time to do everything.)
