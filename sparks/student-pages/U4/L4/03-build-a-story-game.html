Build a Story

In this activity, you will build a group story and learn some vocabulary that shows how we will try to mimic the process on a computer.

Group Game

  1. With a group of at least 4 people (or even with the whole class), try this story-building game. You'll need a piece of paper and something to write with. You'll pass the paper around, and only one person should be looking at the paper at a time (no peeking!). Build the story by writing one word per line, like this:

    Today
    at
    school
    (and so on)

    1. The first person writes down the first three words of a story (one word per line). No one else should see the words. Then, they should fold the paper to hide the first word. In the example above, they would fold the paper so "Today" is hidden, but "at" and "school" can still be seen. Then pass the paper to the second person.
    2. From now on, everyone adds just one word to the story on their turn. The second person sees two words (like "at school" in the example) and makes up the next word (just one word). Then they fold over the first word they saw (in the example, they would fold over "at"). Then pass the paper to the third person.
    3. The third person sees two words (everyone sees just two words) and makes up the next word. They fold the first word they saw (in our example, "school"), and pass the paper to the next person.
    4. Keep going! Each person will always see the last two words of the story so far, add a third word, fold over the first word they saw, and pass the paper. The last person can pass it back to the first person and everyone can take another turn. Do this until you think you've made a decent story.
    5. Once you're ready to stop, unfold the paper and read the story out loud.
    6. Did your story make sense? Was it a good story?

How did you decide on the next word to write? Here's one way to think about it.

: Data, Context, Candidate

Data are basic, raw information (numbers, text, images, audio, etc.).

Context is relevant background information.

Candidates are all the suitable likely choices.

You have a lot of data in your brain, made up of all of the words, sentences, and stories you've ever read, heard, or said.

The two words you saw each time in the story-building game gave you context. The two words (like "at school") were context that helped you think about what word might possibly come next.

There are a lot of candidates for what might come next. Maybe "at school the" or "at school tomorrow" or "at school everyone." Probably not "at school elephant." Your brain thought of candidates and even thought about which ones might be more likely (happen more frequently). Your brain used that information to decide on the next word.

    1. Do you agree? Is this how you decided on the next word to write?
    2. How could we get a computer to go through the same (or a similar) process?
