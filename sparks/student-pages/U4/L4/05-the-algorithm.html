Step 2: Using an algorithm to get the next word

We are still working with our 24-word dataset, chunked into 1-, 2-, and 3-grams. Again, here are the 1-grams, 2-grams, and 3-grams in a table.

We need a set of instructions (the algorithm) for how to use these n-gram chunks to get the “best” next word.

In our program, the user always starts the story. They can start with no words, or write a sentence starter like “Once upon a time,” or entire paragraphs. Whatever the user enters, the program will continue the story, generating one word at a time, using our algorithm. For now, ignore all capitalization and punctuation.

  1. If the user starts the story with: "The cat smells strange"
    1. Just using the n-grams from our 24-word dataset, what word should our program generate as the next word? (Hint: When we limit ourselves to this data, there is one “best” choice.)
    2. What was your process to decide on this word? Take a first pass at writing down an algorithm for your process.

Recall from our game (building the story by folding the paper) that we can think of building a story by looking at context (existing words) to figure out candidates (possible next words) to choose from. The largest “n” for our n-grams determines how large our context can be. Since we have arranged our data up to 3-grams, our largest context is 2 words. (If we had up to 4-grams, our largest context would be 3 words; if we had up to 5-grams, the largest context would be 4 words, and so on.)

If possible, we will match the last two words from the existing story to the first two words of a 3-gram. Then the last word of the matching 3-gram will give us a candidate. If there is more than one matching 3-gram, then we will have more than one candidate. If there is more than one candidate, then we will choose randomly from among the candidates.

  1. If the user starts the story with: "My sister, a curious student"
    1. Using our n-grams, what is the context?
    2. What is/are the candidates?
    3. If there is more than one candidate, choose randomly from among the candidates by flipping a coin, or using a dice, or in Snap!.
  2. If needed, revise or update your algorithm from above.
  3. Keep going with the story.
    1. If your story is now “My sister, a curious student enters” what is your context and candidate(s)?
    2. If your story is now “My sister, a curious student starts” what is your context and candidate(s)?
  4. Generate one more word. At the end of this, your story will be 8 words long.
  5. What do you think should happen if your 2-word context does not match the beginning of any of the 3-grams?

Here are a few more examples of how a user might start the story. In each case, think about what should happen in the algorithm. Click on the example to see a description of what one possible algorithm would do. Update your own algorithm to account for these examples.

What if the user starts the story with: “Yesterday, I saw a”?

The 2-word context is “saw a.” But none of the 3-grams starts with “saw a.” So we go to a 1-word context. The 1-word context is “a.” We look at the 2-grams to get our candidates, and see the 2-grams “a curious” and “a science”. Flip a coin to choose one of these words randomly.


What if the user starts the story with: “Once upon a time”?

The 2-word context is “a time.” But none of the 3-grams starts with “a time.” So we go to a 1-word context. The 1-word context is “time.” But none of the 2-grams starts with “time.” So we cannot use context, and we simply choose a random word from among our 24 1-grams. Each word has an equal 1/24 chance of being chosen.


What if the user starts the story with: “Hello” (or even no words at all)?

"Hello" gives us a 1-word context, so we can turn to our 2-grams. But none of the 2-grams starts with "Hello." (If there are no words at all, then we have no context.) We cannot use context, and we simply choose a random word from among our 24 1-grams. Each word has an equal 1/24 chance of being chosen.
