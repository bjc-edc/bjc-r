The n-gram block gives us a way to make any size n-gram we want. Now we set up our dataset to have all the 1-, 2- and 3-grams together. The dataset with the different-sized n-grams together is our model.

In Unit 4 Lab 2 Activity 4: Training Your ML Model you learned that a model is a recipe that helps a computer find patterns in data. In this project, it is also helpful to think of a model more generally as a stored pattern based on training data that can be used to make decisions in new situations.

Training a model is the same thing as creating a model.

  1. Using up to 3-grams in the project is a choice. You could instead choose to use all the n-grams from 1 to 4, or 1 to 5, or larger.
    1. If you use 1-, 2-, 3-, 4-, and 5-grams, what will be the maximum context length that your algorithm will try to match?
    2. What is the advantage of matching a longer context? (For example, in the game where you folded over the word, what would be the advantage of seeing more of the story that wasn't folded over?)
    3. One potential disadvantage of needing to match a longer context is that you end up with fewer candidates for the next word. Why would you end up with fewer candidates? Why might this be a disadvantage? (To help you think about this, imagine that you had to match a 100-word-long context to get the 101st word in an n-gram. Why does a long context limit your choices for the next word?)

There is no "right" or "best" choice of model. You can try different maximum n-gram sizes to see what happens to your generated story. The model you choose also depends on the quality and size of your training data.

There is also a simplified example in the Snap! project to show how the algorithm works. In the example, we pretend the a user has started the story with "There once lived a." We've chosen our model to go up to 3-grams, so we look to see if there are any matches for the 2-word context "lived a". The block below only keeps the 3-grams that match, and that results in 3 candidates.

In our data, the context "lived a" shows up 3 times. Twice, it appears in the phrase "lived a little" and once it appears in the phrase "lived a penguin." Now, we choose randomly from these three candidates. We are interested in just the last word. When we click the block below, "little" should show up roughly 2/3 of the time and "penguin" should show up roughly 1/3 of the time.
