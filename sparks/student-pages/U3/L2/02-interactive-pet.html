Interactive Pet

In this activity, you'll bring your pet to life using cardboard, paper, and code.

You can do this activity in any order. Choose to work on the physical making of your pet or the code that controls its interactivity.

Add Interactivity

To make your pet interactive, you will need to use at least two different inputs that cause at least two different outputs.

: Input and Output

In computing, the input is the action that tells the computer to do something. The output is the resulting action that occurs after the input command is received.

The micro:bit has different types of inputs: light (bright or dark), button pressed, or certain movement. The output we're currently exploring is displaying a picture on the LED display.

Consider the following inputs and decide which one to use to enhance your pet’s personality or story.

  • light level (like from Lab 1)
  • button A or button B
  • tilt x or tilt y or tilt z

  1. Use a when block to start your script.

  2. Select an input for your interactive pet. Note that you'll need to include an inequality with light level and tilt x. Experiment with different values to trigger the script (100 may not be the right value). For a reminder about inequalities, check out 1.6.2, 1.6.3, and 1.7.

  3. Create an animation using the LED display, wait, and repeat blocks.

Make a Pet

Remember that only part of this project is coding. Use craft materials like paper, markers, and cardboard to bring your pet to life. Think about how you'd like your micro:bit to fit into your project and how your LED display is incorporated.

  1. Make your pet body. Remember to consider how to integrate your micro:bit into your design.

Tell a Story

Now that you've made your interactive pet, document its interactions so that a classmate can know how to interact with it.

Describe your pet, how you interact with it (the inputs and outputs), and a story about your pet.

Example: Meet Orange! He’s a very vocal cat. When the A button is pressed, he meows loudly. When the B button is pressed, he purrs softly.

  1. It's helpful to share your designs with others for design feedback, to share ideas, and to test that your project works as intended. Share your pet with a partner by telling its story of how to interact with it. If something doesn't work as intended, make your desired changes to your design.
