Lab 6: Texting Tricks

At this point, students have learned a variety of functional programming techniques. In Lab 1, they nested blocks to use the output of one block as the input to another. In Lab 2, they used lists to store a selection of possible words, used join to add an "s" to the end of a word, and created new blocks that accept inputs. In Lab 4, they learned how images are stored as a list of lists (a list of pixels each containing a list of color values), and they used the higher-order function map to select a single color value from every pixel in a list. In Lab 5, they nested a more complex series of blocks to encode and decode messages.

Here in Lab 6, students extend their growing facility with functional programming to create and analyze code with nested functions. They learn several new blocks: the relatively simple split block, which students use to divide a text string into a list of letters; the if then else block, which they use to make decisions based on a predicate (testing function); and the higher-order function find first, which identifies the first list item that passes a predicate test.

These more advanced concepts are developed in the playful context of embellishing text with emojis and alternate spellings used in texting and on the Internet, and the lab culminates with a non-programming opportunity to consider issues of bias in the range of emojis available and with students designing an emoji of their own.

Pacing

This lab is designed for 2–11 class periods (70–460 minutes).

  • Daily Activity: Computing in the News 5–15 minutes each day
  • Activity 1: Texting Time: 20–115 minutes
  • Activity 2: L33t Text: 25–90 minutes
  • (Optional) Activity 3: Emojify: 35–75 minutes
  • Activity 4: Emoji Deep Dive: 15–90 minutes

      • emojify: This block takes a string of text as input and reports the same text but with particular words replaced by corresponding emojis.
      • clapify: This block takes a string of text as input and reports the same text but with clap emoji inserted between each of the letters.
      • shorthand: This block takes a string of text as input and reports the same text but with all of the vowels removed.

Students use the reporter form of the if conditional together with a predicate to create a function that takes one letter as input and if the letter the target for their change (such as "e"), then it reports the replacement character (such as "3"), and otherwise reports the input letter. Activity 4: Emoji Deep Dive. 

  • Materials: Activity: Emoji Deep Dive student page
    OLD: Emojify Google Doc
  • Prepare:
    • Review the optional videos and determine if you want to use them with students.
    • Students will need paper and various implements for drawing (pencils and erasers, pens, colored pencils, etc.)
    • There are two optional worksheet pages: Emoji Analysis and Emoji Design. Consider whether you want to use either of these worksheets with students.
  • Learning Goals:
    • Appreciate the history of emojis
    • Consider issues of bias in emojis
    • Design a new emoji and consider its potential meaning and use
  • Activity Plan:
    • A Brief History of Emojis. Students read a quick introduction to the creation of emojis. You may opt to have students read this individually, in small groups, or as a class. (<5 minutes)
    • (Optional) Watch one or more videos included on the student activity page (10–25 minutes):
      • TEDx Talk: Language of the Future on the cultural aspects of emojis and the importance of emojis in text-based conversations by entrepreneur, attorney, inventor and speaker Tracey Pickett (10:37)
      • TEDx Talk: How emoji replaced QWERTY as the world's most popular keyword covering history and issues of bias in emojis by professional emoji historian Jeremy Burge (14:07)
    • Analyzing Emojis. Students choose a word/emoji pair from emojify word (it may be a pair they added, or one they want to add), and discuss the assumptions of the emoji and alternate meanings of the word and emoji. You may choose to bring the class together for a discussion, and you may consider extending the prompts into a writing activity. (5–20 minutes)
    • Designing an Emoji. Students reflect on and then design, discuss, and share an emoji they think the world needs. Depending on student interest, you may choose to extend the sharing into a whole-class discussion about students' designs, their meaning, and potential use. (10–40 minutes)
  • Tips:
    • This activity is a chance for socially oriented and artistically inclined students to shine and for students to bring their own cultures and experiences to the classroom. Make room for their voices.
  • Standards:
    • 2-IC-21: Discuss issues of bias and accessibility in the design of existing technologies.

Correlation with CSTA Standards 

  • 2-AP-13: Decompose problems and subproblems into parts to facilitate the design, implementation, and review of programs. Students should break down problems into subproblems, which can be further broken down to smaller parts. Decomposition facilitates aspects of program development by allowing students to focus on one piece at a time (e.g., getting input from the user, processing the data, and displaying the result to the user). Decomposition also enables different students to work on different parts at the same time. For example, animations can be decomposed into multiple scenes, which can be developed independently.
  • 2-AP-19: Document programs in order to make them easier to follow, test, and debug. Documentation allows creators and others to more easily use and understand a program. Students should provide documentation for end users that explains their artifacts and how they function. For example, students could provide a project overview and clear user instructions. They should also incorporate comments in their product and communicate their process using design documents, flowcharts, and presentations.
  • 2-IC-21: Discuss issues of bias and accessibility in the design of existing technologies. Students should test and discuss the usability of various technology tools (e.g., apps, games, and devices) with the teacher's guidance. For example, facial recognition software that works better for lighter skin tones was likely developed with a homogeneous testing group and could be improved by sampling a more diverse population. When discussing accessibility, students may notice that allowing a user to change font sizes and colors will not only make an interface usable for people with low vision but also benefits users in various situations, such as in bright daylight or a dark room.
