Lab 3: Make Some Noise

This lab covers the basics of visualizing and generating sound waves. Students first create an oscilloscope (first image below) to graph sound intensity over time for data picked up by the computer microphone. They then build a spectrum analyzer (second image below) which graphs the sound intensity of each frequency.

Students then create their own sounds first by generating and playing a set of random intensity values to make white noise, then by repeating a short set of random intensity values to make a note (where pitch depends on the set length, which controls the frequency with which the set repeats); and finally (if there is time) by repeating a short set of specific intensity values to make an instrument.

Pacing

This lab is designed for 2–7 class periods (90–305 minutes). You may opt to focus on some activities in this lab more than others.

  • Activity 1: Visualizing Sound Data: 20–50 minutes
  • Activity 2: Creating an Oscilloscope: 25–55 minutes
  • Activity 3: Creating a Spectrum Analyzer: 15–45 minutes
  • (Optional) Activity 4: Switching Back and Forth: 5–15 minutes
  • Activity 5: Making Noise: 10–20 minutes
  • Activity 6: Making Notes and Instruments: 10–60 minutes

Activity 1: Visualizing Sound Data. 

  • Materials:
    • Visualizing Sound Data student page
  • Prepare:
    • You can right-click the microphone block and choose "help..." to learn more about how the Snap! microphone block works.
      This lab is all about sound. Each pair of students will need a computer with a microphone for the first 4 lab activities. (Speakers and/or headphones are not needed until Activity 5.
    • Before you work on this lab with students, work through the first two pages of the lab yourself to check your microphone and familiarize yourself project. You may also want to load your Snap! project on a student computer and to test out the microphone on a student machine.
  • Learning Goals:
    • Learn about oscilloscopes.
    • Access sound data from a microphone.
    • Learn to use basic Snap! drawing tools.
  • Activity Plan:
    • Learning about oscilloscopes: Students read about oscilloscopes and sound waves. You may choose to take this introduction further such as by demonstrating a completed oscilloscope project, sharing online videos, etc. (5–20 minutes)
    • Exploring the Tools: Students try out the microphone block with various inputs and experiment with Snap! drawing tools. (15–30 minutes)
  • Tips:
    • Your students may be better at troubleshooting microphone issues than you are. If the microphone block isn't detecting sound, here are some things to try:
      • Give it a few seconds and try again. As described on the student page, "You may get zeros as output at first. It can take a moment for Snap! to tell your browser to tell your computer to listen to the microphone."
      • For external microphones, check the physical connection of microphone to the computer.
      • Check system settings to see that the microphone works with the computer.
      • Check the browser settings to see that the microphone works with the browser.
      • As is often recommended for technical issues, try restarting the browser; if that doesn't work, try restarting the computer.
  • Standards:
    • 2-CS-02: Design projects that combine hardware and software components to collect and exchange data.

Activity 2: Creating an Oscilloscope. 

  • Materials:
    • Creating an Oscilloscope student page
  • Learning Goals:
    • Review creating and using global variables.
    • Review list traversal with the for each block.
    • Use the Snap! drawing tools to visualize data.
  • Activity Plan:
    • Unplugged Introduction: In order to clarify the axes of an oscilloscope plot, do this quick unplugged activity where you graph volume intensity over time on the board:
      • Make a plot based on a sound. Draw a coordinate axis on the board with "intensity" as the label on the vertical axis and "time" as the label on the horizontal axis. Have one person (a student or you) sing a pitch (or just say a vowel for a long time: "oooooooooo") and vary the volume (quiet, loud, medium, etc.). As the volume varies, plot the intensity over time. (That is, while moving the pen from left to right across the board, move the pen higher when the sound is louder and lower when the sound is quieter.)
      • Make sound based on a plot. Then do the opposite: draw and label a new axis ("intensity" over "time"), and ask a student (or the class) to sing (or say a vowel) at the volume level you are indicating on a new graph. Start with your pen at the middle of the vertical-axis, and while moving the pen from left to right, draw the line higher or lower to indicate the volume at which they should vocalize.
      This will help students understand what an oscilloscope shows and also helps to distinguish "higher and lower volume" from "higher and lower frequency" which will become relevant in the next activity. (5–10 minutes)
    • Writing Code to Draw One Set of Samples: Students create a global variable to store the width that each sample gets on the stage, create the for each code that will move the sprite to the correct place on the Snap! stage for each volume sample, and discuss how the for each code works. The for each block is the core of the code for this algorithm, and specific changes to this code make the difference between an oscilloscope and a spectrum analyzer, as students will see in the next activity. After students have had a chance to discuss with their partner how the for each code will work, you may opt to facilitate a similar discussion with the whole class. (10–30 minutes)
    • Drawing Microphone Samples Continuously: Students build out the rest of the project, troubleshoot any issues with their code, and add comments to help them remember how their code works. (10–20 minutes)
    • Extensions: Students conduct some simple experiments with the vertical scaling factor. (about 5 minutes)
  • Tips:
    • If the oscilloscope doesn't appear nicely on the Snap! stage, check that the vertical scale factor (the 150 multiplier in the y input to the goto block) is an appropriate value to make the oscilloscope's oscillations both be visible and fit within the Snap! stage (the idea scale value may be vary across devices).
  • Standards:
    • 2-CS-02: Design projects that combine hardware and software components to collect and exchange data.
    • 2-AP-11: Create clearly named variables that represent different data types and perform operations on their values.
    • 2-AP-19: Document programs in order to make them easier to follow, test, and debug.

Activity 3: Creating a Spectrum Analyzer. 

  • Materials:
    • Creating a Spectrum Analyzer student page
  • Prepare:
    • You may find it helpful to work through the contents of this page by yourself before teaching. At a minimum, make sure (using this Teacher Guide together with the student activity pages) that you understand the differences (and the reasons behind the differences) between the for each code for the oscilloscope (shown first below) and for the spectrum analyzer (shown second):
  • Learning Goals:
    • Understand the difference between an oscilloscope and a spectrum analyzer.
    • Modify the oscilloscope code to create a spectrum analyzer.
  • Activity Plan:
    • Reading about spectrum analysis and exploring the microphone block: Students learn the difference between an oscilloscope and a spectrum analyzer and explore the spectrum input to the microphone block. (5–10 minutes)
    • Re-purposing the code from show volume samples to create a new show frequencies block: Students duplicate their existing block and modify the inputs of the for each block and the go to block inside it to make their program draw a intensity-over-frequency plot rather than an intensity-over-time plot. You may wish to wrap up class with a discussion of these changes to ensure that all students successfully updated their code and understand how it works. (10–30 minutes)
    • Extension: Students play music while using the spectrum analyzer. (<5 minutes)
  • Tips:
    • You may find the following prompts helpful for class discussion about the difference between the oscilloscope and spectrum analyzer code:
      • How are an oscilloscope and a spectrum analyzer similar? How are they different?
      • How do these differences appear in the inputs at the top of the for each block for each visualizer?
      • How do the differences appear in the inputs to the go to block?

    • 2-CS-02: Design projects that combine hardware and software components to collect and exchange data.

Activity 5: Making Noise. 

  • Materials:
    • Making Noise student page
  • Prepare:
    • Students will need headphones for this activity. You may need to debug issues with the headphones, such as volume, browser, or system audio input settings.
  • Learning Goals:
    • Reinforce understanding of what sound is and how it can be stored on a computer.
    • Understand the difference in behavior of the pick random block when given integer vs. decimal inputs.
  • Activity Plan:
    • Setting up and discussing how the code will work: Students review what sound is, create and initialize a variable called noise to store intensity values, experiment the inputs to the pick random block, and analyze code that will load random values into the noise variable. (5–10 minutes)
    • Building the code: Students build, run, and debug their code. (5–10 minutes)
  • Standards:
    • 2-CS-02: Design projects that combine hardware and software components to collect and exchange data.
    • 2-AP-11: Create clearly named variables that represent different data types and perform operations on their values.

Activity 6: Making Notes and Instruments. 

  • Materials:
    • Making Notes and Instruments student page
  • Prepare:
    • This activity draws together three products of this lab: oscilloscope, spectrum analyzer, and sound generation. You may wish to develop a complete version of the project (including all the optional exercises from this activity page to share with students before or after they work through the exercises themselves.
  • Learning Goals:
    • Learn that notes are created by repeating a set of volume intensities.
    • (Optional) Learn that different pitches are created by repeating a set of volume intensities of different lengths.
  • Activity Plan:
    • Making a Note: Students create and load values into two new variables: short noise and repeated short noise. Optionally, students may discuss how the code works. If your class has the time and inclination, you may wish to make this a group discussion. (5–20 minutes)
    • Making Other Notes: Optionally, students may create an additional pair of variables to store fewer intensity values, thus creating a higher pitch. (5–10 minutes)
    • Making Other Instruments: Optionally, students may explore using short noises that are not made of random values in order to control the timbre (character) of their sounds. (5–20 minutes)
    • Making a Note: Students play their sound(s) while using their sound visualizers (oscilloscope and the spectrum analyzer) and discuss what they see. (5–10 minutes)
  • Tips:
    • The visualization will be particularly effective if students have created different notes or instruments that they can compare. If there isn't time for this, a demonstration of your own work an produce a similar effect.
  • Standards:
    • 2-CS-02: Design projects that combine hardware and software components to collect and exchange data.
    • 2-AP-11: Create clearly named variables that represent different data types and perform operations on their values.
