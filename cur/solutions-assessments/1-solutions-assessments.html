  • Page 2: Examining Privacy. Here are some possible answers to problem 2, Who is threatening your privacy?
    • Government threats to privacy are the easiest to see, especially in the post-Snowden era. It's important for students to understand that these threats aren't new; that's why the Constitution prohibited unwarranted search or seizure over 200 years ago. On the other hand, they should also understand how dramatically government behavior changed after 9/11. Besides the NSA and the police agencies, government threats include the IRS and more obscure data collection. For example, sales of real estate are public records, and often include Social Security numbers. This wasn't so important when you had to visit the county clerk's office to search the records, but now they're online.
    • The corporate threats everyone talks about are Google and Facebook. But the sharing of information among banks and credit bureaus is equally problematic. Internet Service Providers read the data you send or receive over the net. You can use DuckDuckGo for your web searches instead of Google, but it's hard to avoid using either the phone company or the cable company as your ISP (or your ISP's ISP). A loophole in the laws preventing national police data collection allows the police to buy the data from private companies that do the collection for them.
    • Smartphone owners can record video in public places. This has given rise to a culture of shaming. That can be a good thing when police brutality is documented by a passerby, but incidents like the widespread harassment of "Dog Poop Girl" are troubling. (Since that first example, there have been several more online posting of videos of other dog poop violators.)
  • Page 3: Tanya Rider's Story. Here are some possible answers to problem 2, Is there a way to make a policy that preserves one's right to privacy and also makes sure a person like Tanya Rider gets rescued?
    • How about if the cell phone company was allowed to tell the Red Cross where the missing person's cell phone is? Or the Fire Department?
    • How about if the rule were different for a moving cell phone in a city vs, a stationary cell phone in the middle of nowhere?

eCard Project

If you need to know what an eCard is, look it up.
Have students create an eCard that meets these specifications. In preparation for the AP Create Task, students describe the purpose of their program in 100 words or less and, if necessary, how to use it.

Note: Sound files can be imported into Snap! projects, but which media types work depends on the local computer's browser and security settings. Regarding file size, there is no fixed limit, but large files and limited bandwidth situations can slow or timeout file transfers. Classrooms with limited bandwidth connections may opt to avoid sound importing altogether.
