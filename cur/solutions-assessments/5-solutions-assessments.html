  • Page 6: Heuristic Solutions

    • Exercise 1: Optimization, because finding almost the best solution is often good enough. In many cases, heuristics can make guarantees, e.g., that the solution they find will be within 10% of the cost of the best possible solution. (The hill climbing heuristic explained below this problem on the page doesn't have a guarantee of quality -- it's too simple a technique. If you reach the top of a hill, it's always possible that right around the corner, so to speak, is an enormous mountain.)

      There's something sort of like heuristic methods for decision problems, though. Let's take the example "is 5,825,496,221 a prime number?" There are pretty fast primality tests that can give you a clear No answer, but if you get a Yes there's only, let's say, a 95% probability that the number is prime. You can run the test again, with a different starting point (I'm leaving out the mathematical details...), and get either a guaranteed No or another 95% Yes. Since the test is pretty fast, you can test the candidate prime 100 times, and if all 100 tests say Yes, then there's well over 99% probability that the number is, in fact, prime. People use this kind of primality test in cryptography applications, which are based on finding large primes. If you choose a number that actually isn't prime, your security won't be as good as you think, but still perhaps not a disaster.

      This sort of question is part of the subfield of Analysis of Algorithms, part of theoretical computer science.

    • Exercise 2: Playing chess. It is known that there exists a guaranteed winning strategy for whoever plays White, but nobody knows what that strategy is, and we never will, because working it out takes exponential time in the number of moves. Human chess players have rules for analyzing how good a board position is, using things such as how many more pieces you have than your opponent, which pieces they are, and how they're distributed around the board. The best players have an almost magical ability to glance at a chessboard and know instantly who's winning and what the next move should be. Computers, though, have different strengths. They can analyze every possibility for the next several moves, and thereby generate a large number of possible board configurations after those moves. Then the computer has to use heuristics to find which possible board is best for it, and work backwards to decide where to move.

      Find the biggest item is too fast an algorithm to need heuristics. Finding the lock combination doesn't have good-enough solutions; the lock opens only if you really know the combination! And the best spaghetti sauce is easy: don't put cheese in it.

    • Exercise 3: A problem is undecidable if it's not possible to solve it, even given all the time in the world, and even if they invent some new kind of computer. It isn't at all obvious that there are undecidable problems, but in 1936 two researchers published proofs that they exist. One was Alan Turing, whose name everyone knows because he won World War II. The other, Alonzo Church, was the inventor of Lambda Calculus, the computational theory of functions that gave rise, decades later, to the Lisp programming language and so indirectly to Snap!. Church's paper actually came first, so he's the one whose name everyone should know in this area. The two proofs are constructive, which means they actually produced examples of undecidable problems.

      Another name people should know in this connection is Kurt GÃ¶del, who proved five years earlier that formal axiom systems, such as Euclid's axioms in geometry, can't prove every true statement about the subject they cover. So, there are questions in geometry that Euclid's algorithms can't answer, and the same is true for every axiom system, not just in geometry. What Church and Turing did was to recast this decidability question as a matter of actual computational algorithms rather than axiom systems. They showed that there are things you might want to do with your computer that can't be programmed.

      What's really important for students to understand is how undecidability differs from unreasonable-time algorithms. Some problems that were unreasonable-time 30 years ago will be polynomial-time solvable when quantum computers become really practical. Maybe 30 years from now another entirely new kind of computer will be invented that will be able to solve even more problems in reasonable times that are now impractical. But an undecidable problem will be undecidable forever, no matter what kind of computer comes along.

  • Page 8: Parallelism
    • Exercise 1: This one should be easy. The blocks are run in sequence; each one has to finish before the next one starts. So the script will take 6+4+8 = 18 seconds.

    • Exercise 2: The easiest way to get this problem wrong is to fail to notice the and wait in the broadcast block that starts the program. This block won't be finished until both of the scripts that it starts have finished. Those two scripts take 8 and 4 seconds, and they happen in parallel, so it takes 8 seconds for both to be finished. Only then does the main script start the wait 6 secs. So the total time is 8+6 = 14 seconds.

      Note: Some students may object that Snap! can't really run two threads in parallel; it just simulates that by switching back and forth between threads. This would be true if the threads were doing useful work. But the wait block doesn't actually use up 4 or 6 or 8 seconds of processor time, Instead, the wait block tells Snap! "Take me out of the list of threads wanting to run, until ___ seconds from now." This is why we use wait in these examples, rather than something more practical. Since a waiting thread doesn't use any of the processor time, several can be waiting truly in parallel.

      2. 240 processors take 1.25 minutes; 480 processors take 1.125 minutes. Adding 240 processors saved 1/8 of a minute (7.5 seconds).

    • Exercise 5: This problem basically checks whether they've read the text on the page.     • Exercise 6 (If There Is Time): The programs are the same as in problems 1 and 2. The sequential time (prob. 1) is 18 seconds; the parallel time (prob. 2) is 14 seconds. So the speedup is 18/14. (Some students will feel intuitively that the formula should be parallel/sequential, which would be "what fraction of the sequential time does the parallel version take?" But if we want to measure the speedup due to parallelism, we want an answer greater than 1; "the parallel version is twice as fast," for example. Don't let students say "two times faster," an incoherent neologism.)

Lab 2: Simulations

  • Page 1: Why Use Simulations?
    • Exercise 1:
      • Impractical: Add a lane to the highway and see if that improves the traffic jams. What if it's a carpool lane? Etc.
      • Impossible: Suppose gravity followed an inverse-cube law instead of inverse-square. What would planetary orbits look like? A more real-life example is that physicists want to know about the nature of physical laws during the first fraction of a second after the creation of the universe.
      • Unethical: We are writing these solutions during the COVID-19 pandemic, and so there are many real-life examples of things researchers want to know that are hard to find out without deliberately infecting volunteers, such as how long an infected person remains contagious, and what fraction of infected people develop symptoms.
    • Exercise 2: Simulations allow performing quasi-experiments in the computer model, and the results can indicate likely results if the same experiment were made in the real world. Visual display of information can help people understand the phenomena; for example, a computer can display a planetary orbit so the experimenter can see the result rather than just seeing equations describing the result.
      • Pro: Computers can model the impractical, impossible, or unethical situations that would otherwise not be available for experiments. Even when something can be done in the real world, computer modeling is generally much cheaper and faster, so many experiments can be made instead of only a few. (For example, auto body designs are tested in simulated wind tunnels many times, until the designers think they have the optimal shape, and only then do they actually build a physical car and try it in a physical wind tunnel.)
      • Con: There's only one big problem with computer modeling, but it's a crucial one: The simplifications made in building the computer model may be more important than the developers think, and so the results may not really be correct. This is especially likely when modeling very complex systems, in which a small error in one piece of the model may have much larger effects in other pieces. A related problem is that sometimes the model-builders make simplifications that they know are importantly wrong, but they don't know how to build a better one. For example, many economists assume that everyone in a society has perfect access to relevant information, and always behaves rationally. This is so wrong that their conclusions aren't very convincing to anyone who looks at a real economy. The risk of oversimplified assumptions is particularly great in social sciences, where prejudice based on race, sex, etc. can affect the simulation. (Prejudice can affect real-world experiments, too, but at least the assumptions are more visible and can be criticized.)

Lab 4: Unsolvable and Undecidable Problems

  • Page 1: Proof by Contradiction
    • Exercise 1: If Adam is a False-Teller, then they are not from the same family, and so Eve is a Truthteller. If Adam is a Truthteller, then they are from the same family, and still Eve is a Truthteller. Either way, Eve is a Truthteller, but there is no way to know for sure which family Adam is from.
      1. Yes. Truthteller
      2. No.
    • Exercise 2: Derek says yes. If he is from the Truthteller family, then he'll tell the truth and say yes. If he is from the False-Teller family, then he'll lie and say yes.
    • Exercise 3: Derek says no. If he is from the Truthteller family, then he'll tell the truth and say no. If he is from the False-Teller family, then he'll lie and say no.
    • Exercise 4: Betsy's statement is a paradox; it is a logical contradiction that is neither true nor false. If the statement "The statement I'm making right now is false" is true, then is has to be false because the true statement says it is false. However, if the statement is false, then because it says it is false, it must be true. Neither makes sense.
    • Exercise 5: Questions that will help you determine whether a person is a Truthteller or a False-Teller are those that the two will answer differently.
      1. For example:
        • Are you a person?
        • Is the sky blue?
        • Do we live on the sun?
        • Is the United States in Europe?
      2. The following table shows how Adam would answer each question if he were a Truthteller or a False-Teller:
        Question Truthteller False-Teller
        Are you a person? Yes No
        Is the sky blue? Yes No
        Do we live on the sun? No Yes
        Is the United States in Europe? No Yes
    • Exercise A (Take It Further): Max's statement that "Min and I are both liars!" is not a contradiction, and it is not indeterminate; it is definitely resolvable. If Max were a Truthteller, then he would not say that they were both liars. So, the only possibility is that Max is a False-Teller, in which case, the statement must be a lie; so they are not both False-Tellers, and Min must be a Truthteller. Thus, the statement is definitely resolvable.
  • Page 2: An Undecidable Problem
    • Exercise 1: These discussions aren't meant to be handed in; instead, you might have a whole-class discussion after the partners have a chance for their small-group discussions.
      1. In a proof by contradiction, you start by assuming the opposite of what you're trying to prove, and from there you derive a contradiction. You prove that the opposite can't be true, which means the original goal must be true. (By the way, although it's an unusual position today, around 1900 there was a strong group of mathematicians who rejected proof by contradiction. So if your students have trouble with the idea, they're in good company!)
      2. The halts? function takes a reporter (that is, a function; for example, f) and an input value (an argument; for example, x) and reports true if and only if the computation of f(x) will report a value in finite time. In other words, halts? is (supposedly) able to detect an infinite loop in any program. (Note that this proof would be impossible in a programming language in which procedures can't be used as data, that is, in which a function couldn't be used as input to another function, such as inputting f into halts?. The Halting Theorem would still be true, but proving it would be a lot harder—as it was for Turing.)
      3. We construct a procedure tester so that it reports a value if and only if halts? says that f(f) loops forever for some given function f but also so that tester loops forever if halts? says that f(f) reports a value. So, whatever f does, tester does the opposite. Then we call tester on itself: tester(tester). Whatever halts? says that tester will do, tester will be forced to actually do the opposite. That's a contradiction, and so an infallible halts? can't exist.

Lab 5: Computing in War

  • Page 1: War and Technology
    • Exercise 1: If you have a Super Soaker and your friend just has a squirt gun, you will win!

    • Exercise 2: Offensive technologies: Swords, knives, chemical explosives (a long list of them), fishing line (as garotte), bombs, guns, bayonets, cannons, catapults, fire, poison (e.g., in water source), Trojan horse, regular horses, malware, nuclear bombs, gas (poisonous, corrosive), ships, airplanes, missiles, satellites, drones, lasers, lots more.

      Defensive technologies: Walls, armor, shields, various spying technologies, vast stockpile of offensive weapons used as deterrent, alarms, radar, more.

    • Exercise 3: Metal-clad ships were fairly new at the time of the Civil War. Wooden ships were not only easier for cannonball to penetrate, but very vulnerable to fire. The South were first (1861) to deploy an ironclad, but by 1862 the North had seven smaller ironclads, as well as the large USS Monitor. Seeing an urgent need to match the Monitor, the South raised the scuttled (deliberately sunk) USS Merrimack, clad it in iron, and rechristened it the CSS Virginia. The first battle (worldwide) between two ironclads was the Battle of Hampton Roads, in which the Virginia was destroying several wooden ships of the North until the barely-built Monitor rushed to the scene and drove off the Virginia. Both ships survived the encounter, but each was destroyed later in the war. It quickly became clear that a wooden ship could not survive battle with an ironclad.

      The gunpowder that provided the propellant force for rifles until the 1880s filled battlefields with thick black smoke, making it hard to tell allies from enemies. Bright colored uniforms helped improve visibility. Improved weaponry reduced the smoke problem, at which point it became more important to keep one's own soldiers hidden from the enemy. Dull colors that blended with the landscape then became the standard.

      Before airplanes were used in warfare, there were generally well-defined battlefields, and rules of war disallowed attacks on civilians. But military airplanes don't have riflemen; they have bombs, which kill people over a wide area. A civilian anywhere near a battlefield could no longer rely on being protected by wearing civilian clothing. And once air war became common, it didn't take long until bombs were dropped on cities. Today there is really no such thing as a "noncombatant."

  • Page 2: Computers and War (The exercises on this page are mostly "Look up..." and so the "solutions" are in your search engine.)
  • Page 3: Military Technology in Everyday Life
    • Exercise 1: All military, of course:
      1. The story of the Manhattan Project is well known. Physicists understood the theoretical possibility of a chain reaction, but actually achieving one took an enormous amount of effort and money, made available because of the fear that the Nazis would solve the problem first.
      2. GPS was a Defense Department project, motivated originally by the desire to get very precise coordinates for bombings of specific buildings, but also used to allow battlefield officers to know precisely which soldier is where despite the chaos of battle.
      3. The Internet was meant to ensure high-reliability communication through network redundancy, and to allow more efficient use of communication circuits through packet switching, which allows many conversations over the same circuit. Many people believe that the technology was developed specifically for the sake of a network that would survive a nuclear attack on the United States, but this is apparently not the case. (For one thing, few computers would survive the strong electromagnetic pulse that is a side effect of a nuclear explosion.)
    • Exercise 2: The Roomba automatic vacuum cleaner started life as a military robot. Drones, first used as weapons, are now widely available for news coverage and even as toys. Radar, developed to detect enemy missiles or vehicles, is now used to police highway speed (unpopular, but life-saving) and to enable adaptive cruise control (which slows down your car if it get too near to the car in front of it). Several weapons, especially non-fatal ones, have moved from military to police use, e.g., tear gas. The technology to develop vaccines also serves to develop biological weapons (although they are outlawed by international treaties). Night vision technology has many civilian uses but its export is regulated as a munition because soldiers use it for night attacks.
    • Exercise 3: This question is deliberately phrased provocatively. The first part is rhetorical; of course killing someone is worse than deleting their computer files. But the second is not rhetorical. It's important to understand that institutions have a character of their own, separate from that of the people who make up the institution. Nobody at the ACM is in favor of killing people! But their principles for the conduct of members are, at best, silent on the question, while voluble about lesser harms.
    • Exercise 4: This is a question about ethics, not about computer science, and there is room for disagreement. But students who argue that the United States should have the best possible weapons, because we're the good guys, should read Wikipedia's list of wars involving the United States. Compare the two occasions in all of its history when the United States was invaded militarily and had to defend itself (War of 1812, Pearl Harbor) with the countless invasions of other countries by the US. (This is not to suggest that the US is especially evil! But it's not especially benevolent either. It's a country like many others.)

Lab 6: Tic-Tac-Toe

  • Page 1: Review Your Tic-Tac-Toe Project
    • Exercise 2: What do these blocks do?
      1. Board is a variable containing a list of nine items, one for each square of the board, each of which is an X, an O, or the square's number if it's vacant.
      2. Possible winning triples reports the same list every time it's called: eight items, each of which is a list of three numbers, representing three squares that form a vertical, horizontal, or diagonal line.
      3. Status of all winning triples reports a list that's like the one from possible winning triples except that if a square is occupied, its square number is replaced by an X or O wherever it appears.
      4. Won? takes a letter X or O as input, and reports True if and only if that player has won the game (has three squares in a row).
    • Exercise 3: Board is used to ask for the status (X, O, or vacant) of one individual square; status of all winning triples is used to ask whether any of the eight possible winning vertical, horizontal, or diagonal lines has some particular status (e.g., X has won, O can win on the next move, etc.).
    • Exercise 4: Each square on the stage is covered by a sprite, which is initially a white square but changes its costume to X or O when played. When you click in a square, that one sprite runs its when I am clicked script. Each sprite knows which square it's in (using a sprite-local variable), so in addition to changing its costume, it can change its corresponding item in the board list.
