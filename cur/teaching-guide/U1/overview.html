Class Structure and Content

The content is presented in weeks, assuming that classes meet around 4 hours per week. Class time generally falls into one of five categories, plus homework: NEED TO EDIT!!
  • Opener, a 5-minute activity to start the class. There are four kinds of Openers:
    • Computing in the News
      Students are presented with and discuss a current news story about computing. Students gain a greater sense of the relevance of computing in society, are exposed to complex social issues, and learn to read news stories with an informed and critial eye.
    • Make a Prediction
      Students predict the result of a piece of code. Students reason through the logic of code. Teachers might use these to introduce a new concept or address a common misconception.
    • Aww, Snap!
      Students are presented with code containing an error and must debug it. Students gain experience with debugging and must make sense of code. Teachers might use these to point to a common pitfall or teach a debugging strategy.
    • Snap Judgment
      Students write down their view in response to a prompt about a Social Implications topic. This is not meant to be polished writing, or even take a view that students firmly believe. The purpose is to engage students and support written communication skills. Throughout the year, students should develop their ability to state a position and provide evidence to support it.
  • Research, 10-15 minutes in which students learn about a topic by reading an article or looking up information online
  • Sharing Student Work, a 10-15 minute class discussion in which students see the posted work of others to talk about different problem-solving approaches and common pitfalls, or just to showcase students' creativity
  • Discussion, a 25-30 minute activity in which students might have a discussion or debate about a Social Implication or Big Idea of Computing
  • Lab, 25-40 minutes during which students generally work in pairs through online lab materials on the student pages
  • Homework There are approximately two homework assignments provided per week.
    • Depending on your class, it might make sense to give more or less homework. Many of the Opener or Research activities can be used for homework assignments as well.

Graphic Design Elements of the BJC Student Materials

The BJC curriculum has several design elements, each there to serve a specific purpose:

Yellow boxes are for optional reading.
  1. As the name imples, these are activities for students to try in class.
  2. As much as possible, students (in their pairs) should complete all of these problems.
Alphie: These are scripts for skits that model good thinking.
Betty: They are designed to be read out loud in class
Students should act out the stage directions instead of reading them aloud.
Gammie: Mix it up! Students can read these dialogues in small groups, in front of the class (maybe even have two groups read in turn to increase retention), or assign it as a homework assignment and have students create a video of their reading.
These orange boxes are designed to highlight important information.
  1. These problems are optional.
  2. Use them as differentiation for students who move faster.
  3. Or assign them for homework.
