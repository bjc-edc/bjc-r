Pacing:

These materials were adapted from a Univeristy of California, Berkeley one semester course. To help AP high school classes stay on track for the year, this high school curriculum is organized into 2 semesters of 18 weeks each and assumes that classes meet around 4 hours per week. As this curriculum is under development, the week-by-week timing should be considered as a guideline for long-term rather than day-by-day planning.

Class time should be used for both Programming Labs, in which students pair program in snap, and Social Implciations Labs, in which students read, discuss, and write about issues of computing in society using the Blown to Bits book (which can be purchased in hard-copy, but is also available online for free) and various online articles.

Additional Problems/Activities: Each week of Programming or Social Implications lab materials include several activities for class discussion. These may include:

  • Opener, a 5-minute activity to start the class. There are four kinds of Openers:
    • Computing in the News
      Students are presented with and discuss a current news story about computing. Students gain a greater sense of the relevance of computing in society, are exposed to complex social issues, and learn to read news stories with an informed and critial eye.
    • Make a Prediction
      Students predict the result of a piece of code. Students reason through the logic of code. Teachers might use these to introduce a new concept or address a common misconception.
    • Aww, Snap!
      Students are presented with code containing an error and must debug it. Students gain experience with debugging and must make sense of code. Teachers might use these to point to a common pitfall or teach a debugging strategy.
    • Snap Judgment
      Students write down their view in response to a prompt about a Social Implications topic. This is not meant to be polished writing, or even take a view that students firmly believe. The purpose is to engage students and support written communication skills. Throughout the year, students should develop their ability to state a position and provide evidence to support it.
  • Research, 10-15 minutes in which students learn about a topic by reading an article or looking up information online
  • Sharing Student Work, a 10-15 minute class discussion in which students see the posted work of others to talk about different problem-solving approaches and common pitfalls, or just to showcase students' creativity
  • Discussion, a 25-30 minute activity in which students might have a discussion or debate about a Social Implication or Big Idea of Computing
  • Lab, 25-40 minutes during which students generally work in pairs through online lab materials on the student pages

Homework Assignments: Assigning homework is optional but recommended. Depending on the group and students' access to the internet, teachers should select assignments from the bank of suggestions corresponding to each week. There are approximately two assignments offered per week, and these may include reading, writing, or programming tasks. Depending on your class, it might make sense to give more or less homework. Many of the Additional Problems/Activities and also the Take it Further problems in the Programming Labs can be used for homework assignments as well.

Prepare:

Ideally by the first day of school, teachers:

  1. Have read the College Board AP Computer Science Priciples Curriculum Framework;
  2. Have worked through most of the Beauty and Joy of Computing Student Programming Labs;
  3. Are comfortable with snap programming (see also: snap manual);
  4. Have read Blown to Bits by Hal Abelson, Ken Ledeen, and Harry Lewis;
  5. Have read this Introduction to the Teacher Guide;
  6. Have read the Unit 1 Teaching Guide; and have familiarized themselves with the Teacher Guide for the Remaining Units (2-8).

Other ways to prepare:

  • Become a Member of the AP Teacher Community, an online discussion forum for teachers to share CS Principles news, Instructional Activities for Big Ideas, and Support for New Teachers.
  • Check out csteachingtips.org, tips from computer science teachers such as:
    • Have students sign a "Computer Use Agreement" before giving them access to machines to hold them accountable for using computers responsibly.
    • Encourage students to talk to each other to decrease the burden on the teacher and to emulate industry work environments. (This page includes a link to a Khan Academy article on pair programming. Pair Programming should be practiced regularly in BJC classrooms.)

Graphic Design Elements

The BJC student materials have several visual design elements, each to serve a specific purpose:

Yellow boxes contain additional information for optional reading.
  1. As the name implies, these are activities for students to try in class.
  2. As much as possible, students should (in their pairs) complete all of these problems.
Alphie: These are scripts for short skits that model good computational thinking.
Betty: They are designed for students to read aloud in class.
Students should act out the italic stage directions instead of reading them out loud.
Gammie: Mix it up! Students can read these dialogues in small groups or in front of the class (maybe even have two groups read in turn to increase retention), or teachers can assign them as a homework assignment and have students record a video or podcast of their reading.
These orange boxes are designed to highlight important information and ways of thinking.
  1. These problems are optional.
  2. Use them as differentiation for students who move faster.
  3. Or assign them for homework.
