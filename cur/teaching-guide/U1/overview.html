Introduction to Teacher Guide

Welcome to the Teacher Guide (first version) for the Beauty and Joy of Computing (BJC) AP Computer Science Principles (CSP) course. This guide is designed to support high school CSP teachers with guidance on timing and activities for using the BJC student materials to teach important ideas of programming and the social implications of computing and to prepare students for the AP CSP Exam that launches Spring 2017. Please offer feedback on these teacher and student materials; your feedback is vital to their improvement.

Equity and Inclusion

A primary goal of the course is to make rigorious computer science accessible to all students, especially to populations traditionally underrepresented in the field. Please take note of unmet needs for your students and any ideas you have for meeting them better, and communicate these with the research team via the blue feedback button and/or research participant surveys.

AP Computer Science Principles

BJC is one of several curricula that satisfy the framework for the new (starting 2016-17) AP CS Principles exam. Most AP courses have specific curricula, and are taught in broadly similar ways at every high school where they are offered. This one is different because it is meant to be equivalent to a college breadth course, rather than a first course for CS majors, and there is no uniformity in how colleges teach such a "CS 0" course. In particular, the exam is meant to be "language agnostic," meaning that any programming language can be used in teaching it.

The course was developed as part of an effort to attract historically underrepresented groups to computer science. It's an AP course because, as a tactical matter, it's much easier to get high schools across the country to introduce an AP course than a non-AP course for which there is no national high school standard curriculum. But always keep in mind that we are hoping to attract to the course kids who don't ordinarily view themselves as "AP kids."

The College Board framework gives minimum standards for each of the "Big Ideas" and "Computational Thinking Practices" listed to the right, but each curriculum has its own emphases. BJC puts a heavy emphasis on Programming and Global Impact, with comparatively small units on Data and the Internet â€” and even in those units, we try to approach the topic through programming activities. (Although we don't have specific units on Creativity or Abstraction, we do emphasize those topics throughout the programming curriculum. Similarly, for those Practices that talk about "artifacts," our emphasis is on programs themselves, and the results of running self-written programs, as the artifacts.)

Course Pacing Guide:

This Beauty and Joy of Computing high school course is an draft adaptation of a popular UC Berkeley one semester introuction to computer science, CS10. This Teaching Guide is designed help high school teachers prepare students for the AP Computer Science Principles Exam first available Spring 2017. To help AP classes stay on track, BJC is organized into 8 units designed to be split evenly across 2 semesters of 18 weeks each and assumes that classes meet around 4 hours per week and students ideally do 1 hours of BJC homework a week. As this curriculum is under development, the week-by-week timing should be considered as a guideline for long-term rather than day-by-day planning.

Student Materials:

  • Labs: This curriculum includes two kinds of labs:
      Blown to Bits can be purchased in hard-copy, and it is also available online for free.
    • Programming Labs, in which students practice pair programming in snap, are included in each of the 8 units.
    • Social Implications Labs, in which students read, discuss, and write about issues of computing in society using the Blown to Bits book and various online articles. Social Implications Labs are included in the first 4 units.
  • Getting Started: Many labs include a "Getting Started" page that let students experience the ideas of the upcoming lab before formalizing them. Getting Started lessons are designed for two purposes:
    • to give students a preview, in simple and transparent contexts, of upcoming ideas.
    • to give teachers a sense of what prior knowledge students bring to the lab.
    These Getting Started pages are meant to be done by students with minimal instruction from the teacher. Students will likely not be able to answer every question—that's part of the design. The whole idea is to give students a preview of coming attractions and to let you know what students are bringing to the lab in terms of what they already know.
  • Assignments: Programming and social implications projects done outside of class are recommended. These may be Hour(s) of Code (in Unit 1), unit project suggestions, weekly assignment suggestions (reading, writing, and programming), or even lab materials (especially if done in pairs). Working outside of class provides more class time for discussion and support. Consider assigning about 1-2 hours of BJC homework each week. Experiment to find the balance that works for your students.

Supporting Students

Both programming and thinking critically about culture are challenging pursuits. Encourage students to strive for excellence in their code and their anthropology. Help them find their own beauty and joy in computing. Adapt programming and social implications assignments to include and inspire students' interests.

The following are included for each week of the year:

  • Lab Notes: For each lab in the student materials, the Teacher Guide includes a purpose statement, teacher preparation tips, an overview of the work students will undertake, additional tips on presenting the ideas, what to be on the lookout for, and correlations to the AP CS Principles Computational Thinking Practices and Essential Knowledge, and addtional resources.
  • Additional Activities: Each week of Programming or Social Implications lab materials include several activities to support class discussion. These may be pieces of code to analyze or debug or a discussion or writing prompt about current events in computing.
  • Additional Assignments: Assigning homework is optional but recommended. Depending on the group and students' access to the internet, teachers should select assignments from the bank of suggestions corresponding to each week. There are approximately two assignments offered per week, and these may include reading, writing, or programming tasks. Depending on your class, it might make sense to give more or less homework. Many of the Additional Problems/Activities and also the Take it Further problems in the Programming Labs can be used for homework assignments as well.
