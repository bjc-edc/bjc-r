Introduction to Teacher Guide

Welcome to the first version of the Beauty and Joy of Computing AP Computer Science Principles Teacher Guide. This guide is designed to support high school AP Computer Science Principles (CSP) teachers in preparing students for the AP CSP Exam that launches Spring 2017. Please read (and offer feedback on) these teacher and student materials; teacher feedback is vital to their improvement.

Accessibility

A primary goal in the development of this course is to make rigorious computer science accessible to all students, especially to populations traditionally underrepresented in the field. Please take note of unmet needs for your students and any ideas you have for meeting them better, and communicate these with the research team via the blue feedback button and/or research participant surveys.

BJC Course Structure/Pacing:

These materials are an early adaptation of a popular UC Berkeley one semester course, CS10, into a high school AP Computer Science Principles course. To help AP classes stay on track for the year, this curriculum is organized into 8 Units designed to be split across 2 semesters of 18 weeks each and assumes that classes meet around 4 hours per week. As this curriculum is under development, the week-by-week timing should be considered as a guideline for long-term rather than day-by-day planning.

Fall Term:

  • Unit 1: Introduction to snap (4 weeks)
  • Unit 2: Conditionals, Abstraction, and Testing (4 weeks)
  • Quarter 1 Project (1 week)
  • Unit 3: Lists (4 weeks)
  • Unit 4: Algorithm Complexity (5 weeks)
Spring Term:
  • AP Explore Task (2 weeks)
  • Unit 5: Recursion (2 weeks)
  • Unit 6: Recursive Reporters (3 weeks)
  • AP Create Task (3 weeks)
  • Unit 7: Big Data (2 weeks)
  • AP Prep and Review (1 week)
  • AP Exam (1 week)
  • Unit 8: Python and Final Project (4 weeks)

Is is important to keep classes on track for the AP Exam, which begins in Spring 2017 and is scheduled into this course before Unit 8. Unit 7 is crucial to success on the AP Exam, so teachers who are pressed for time are encouraged to postpone all of Unit 6 and ensure that Unit 7 is solid before the exam. Teachers should take note of their pacing and ideas for improvement for the next year both for their own planning and to offer feedback to the curriculum research and development team.

Daily Class Structure

  • Openers, 5-minute activities, start the class.
  • Discussions, a 25-30 minute activity before or after lab time, in in which students have a discussion or debate about a Social Implication or Big Idea of Computing.
  • Labs comprise the remainder of class time, 25-40 minutes during which students generally work in pairs through online lab materials on the student pages. This curriculum includes two kinds of labs:
      Blown to Bits can be purchased in hard-copy, but it is also available online for free.
    • Progamming Labs, in which students practice pair programming in snap, are included in each of the 8 units.
    • Social Implications Labs, in which students read, discuss, and write about issues of computing in society using the Blown to Bits book and various online articles. Social Implications Labs are included in the first 4 units.

Graphic Design Elements

The BJC student materials have several visual design elements, each to serve a specific purpose:

Yellow boxes contain additional information for optional reading.
  1. As the name implies, these are activities for students to try in class.
  2. As much as possible, students should (in their pairs) complete all of these problems.
Alphie: These are scripts for short skits that model good computational thinking.
Betty: They are designed for students to read aloud in class.
Students should act out the italic stage directions instead of reading them out loud.
Gammie: Mix it up! Students can read these dialogues in small groups or in front of the class (maybe even have two groups read in turn to increase retention), or teachers can assign them as a homework assignment and have students record a video or podcast of their reading.
These orange boxes are designed to highlight important information and ways of thinking.
  1. These problems are optional.
  2. Use them as differentiation for students who move faster.
  3. Or assign them for homework.
