Teacher Guide home

Welcome to the Teacher Guide (first version) for the Beauty and Joy of Computing (BJC) AP Computer Science Principles (CSP) course. This guide is designed to support high school CSP teachers with timing and activities for the BJC student materials to teach important ideas of programming and the social implications of computing and to prepare students for the AP CSP Exam that launches Spring 2017. Please offer feedback on these teacher and student materials; your feedback is vital to their improvement.

Equity and Inclusion

A primary goal of this course is to attract new students, especially those traditionally underrepresented in CS, to the joys and life opportunities that come with programming and computer science, and to make rigorous computer science accessible to all. Please take note of any ways this curriculum does not meet the needs of your students and any ideas you have for meeting them better, and tell the research team by using the blue feedback button and/or research participant surveys.

AP Computer Science Principles

BJC is one of several curricula that satisfy the framework for the new (starting 2016-17) AP CS Principles exam. Most AP courses have specific curricula, and are taught in broadly similar ways at every high school where they are offered. This one is different because it is meant to be equivalent to a college breadth course, rather than a first course for CS majors, and there is no uniformity in how colleges teach such a "CS 0" course. In particular, the exam is meant to be "language agnostic," meaning that any programming language can be used in teaching it.

The course was meant to attract historically underrepresented groups to computer science. It's an AP course because, as a tactical matter, it's much easier to get high schools across the country to introduce an AP course than a non-AP course for which there is no national high school standard curriculum. But keep in mind that the goal is to attract students who don't see themselves as "computer geeks" and may not view themselves as "AP kids."

The College Board framework gives minimum standards for each of the "Big Ideas" and "Computational Thinking Practices" listed to the right, but each curriculum has its own emphasis. BJC puts a heavy emphasis on Programming and Global Impact. See also the College Board AP Computer Science Principles Curriculum Framework.

Student Materials:

  • Labs: This curriculum includes two kinds of labs:
      Blown to Bits can be purchased in hard-copy, and it is also available online for free.
    • Programming Labs, in which students practice pair programming in snap, are included in each of the eight units.
    • Social Implications Labs, in which students read, discuss, and write about issues of computing in society using the Blown to Bits book and various online articles. Social Implications Labs are heaviest in the first four units, to precede the AP Explore task.
  • Getting Started: Many labs include a "Getting Started" page that let students experience the ideas of the upcoming lab before formalizing them. Getting Started lessons are designed for two purposes:
    • to give students a preview, in simple and transparent contexts, of upcoming ideas.
    • to give teachers a sense of what prior knowledge students bring to the lab.
    These Getting Started pages are meant to be done by students with minimal instruction from the teacher. Students will likely not be able to answer every question in their pairs—that's part of the design. Be sure students understand that before they start the lab! Allow the sticking points to launch the lab topics that follow.
  • Assignments: Programming and social implications work done outside of class are recommended for a minimum of 1 hour a week, ideally more. These may include unit investigations, weekly assignment suggestions (reading, writing, and programming), or even lab materials (especially if done in pairs). Working outside of class provides more class time for discussion and support. Consider assigning about 1-2 hours of BJC homework each week. We acknowledge that some teachers in some schools will not be able to assign even 1 hour a week. Experiment to find the balance that works for your students.
  • Assessments: Many labs include multiple choice, formative, ungraded "Self Checks" (suggestions on effective student feedback messages appreciated). Don't feel you have to collect and review these. Regular projects and formal exams offer more summative feedback for teachers and students.
