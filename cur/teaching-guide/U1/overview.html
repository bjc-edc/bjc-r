Introduction to Teacher Guide

Welcome to the first version of the Beauty and Joy of Computing AP Computer Science Principles Teacher Guide. This guide is designed to support high school AP Computer Science Principles (CSP) teachers in preparing students for the AP CSP Exam that launches Spring 2017. Please read (and offer feedback on) these teacher and student materials; teacher feedback is vital to their improvement.

Broadening Participation

A primary goal in the development of this course is to make rigorous computer science accessible to all students, especially to populations traditionally underrepresented in the field. Please take note of unmet needs for your students and any ideas you have for meeting them better, and communicate these with the research team via the blue feedback button and/or research participant surveys.

Course Pacing Guide:

This Beauty and Joy of Computing high school course is an draft adaptation of a popular UC Berkeley one semester introuction to computer science, CS10. This Teaching Guide is designed help high school teachers prepare students for the AP Computer Science Principles Exam first available Spring 2017. To help AP classes stay on track, BJC is organized into 8 units designed to be split evenly across 2 semesters of 18 weeks each and assumes that classes meet around 4 hours per week and students ideally do 1 hours of BJC homework a week. As this curriculum is under development, the week-by-week timing should be considered as a guideline for long-term rather than day-by-day planning.

Daily Class Structure

Every day starts with an Opener, a 5-minute activity to start the class. (These are included in the Additional Problems/Activities included in each week.) The remainder of class time is used for:

  • Discussions, a 5-20 minute activity before or after lab time, in which students have a discussion or debate about a Social Implication or Big Idea of Computing.
  • Labs comprise the remainder of class time, 25-40 minutes during which students generally work in pairs through online lab materials on the student pages.

The following are included for each week of the year:

  • Lab Notes: For each lab in the student materials, the Teaching Guide includes a purpose statement, teacher preparation tips, an overview of the work students will undertake, additional tips on presenting the ideas, what to be on the lookout for, and correlations to the AP CS Principles Computational Thinking Practices and Essential Knowledge, and addtional resources.
  • Additional Activities: Each week of Programming or Social Implications lab materials include several activities to support class discussion. These may be pieces of code to analyze or debug or a discussion or writing prompt about current events in computing.
  • Additional Assignments: Assigning homework is optional but recommended. Depending on the group and students' access to the internet, teachers should select assignments from the bank of suggestions corresponding to each week. There are approximately two assignments offered per week, and these may include reading, writing, or programming tasks. Depending on your class, it might make sense to give more or less homework. Many of the Additional Problems/Activities and also the Take it Further problems in the Programming Labs can be used for homework assignments as well.
