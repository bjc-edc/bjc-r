Programming Lab 5: Block Types and Calculations

Students continue experimenting with and combining blocks as they formalize their understanding of sprite state (position and direction) and blocks. Students learn to differentiate between command blocks (including loops and conditionals), reporter blocks, and predicates (including Boolean constant blocks). After most of a unit exploring a variety of blocks, students now learn how block shapes relate to their functionality.

Students also explore a variety of math reporters (from the Operators palette) including basic operations, round, mod, and logical operators (true, false, and, or, and not) and refine their understanding of the random block.

Pacing

The 4 lab pages could be split across one or two days (35-55 minutes). Expected times to complete follow:

  • 5-10 minutes on Sprite Position and Direction
  • 10-15 minutes on Expressions and Operations
  • 10-15 minutes on Predicates and Logic
  • 10-15 minutes on Random Block Self Check

Students will:

  • Connect the idea of sprite position and direction in Snap! to the conventions for referencing position and direction in mathematics.
  • Formalize their language and understanding of several different types of blocks in snap.
  • Broaden and refine their understanding of the mathematics of computer science including basic arithmetic operations, the round block, the mod block, the random block, and logical operators.
  • Analyze code with nested operations.

Tips:

  • Remind students that they can run scripts by clicking on them, including reporters, which will report their result in a speech balloon next to the block.
  • Some teachers have complained about the use of compass directions rather than Cartesian directions. Here's some history:
    • This tradition started in Logo in the 1980s and was inherited by Scratch, then by us.
    • In Logo, the turtle had the arrowhead shape that's our default, and pointed up by default. This is the right thing for fractal trees, and as good as anything for other turtle geometry exercises like the ones in BJC.
    • Scratch shifted the focus from turtle graphics to animation of characters, so sprites have costumes, such as their trademark cat, that move mostly horizontally. So they ended up with Logo's compass directions, but with a default direction of East (90 degrees).
    • The choice of direction measure in Snap! is motivated mostly by the desire to be compatible with Scratch, so that we have access to their 10 million uploaded projects.

  • Some vocabulary (for you, not to impose on students except by example):
    • A command is a single Lego-brick-shaped block, without inputs.
    • A reporter is a single oval-shaped block, without inputs.
    • A predicate is a single hexagonal block, without inputs.
    • An instruction is a command block along with its input expressions.
    • An expression is a reporter block along with its input expressions.
    • A Boolean expression is a predicate block along with its input expressions.
    • A Boolean value is or . (Not the text strings "true" and "false"; not 0 and 1.)
    So a reporter or a predicate is a function; an expression is a function applied to arguments.
