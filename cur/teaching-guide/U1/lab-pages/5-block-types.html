Students continue experimenting with and combining blocks as they formalize their understanding of sprite state (position and direction) and blocks; learn to differentiate between command blocks (including loops and conditionals), reporter blocks, and predicates (and the Boolean constants true and false); learn about computer mathematics including modulo, binary numerals, random numbers, logic. After most of a unit exploring a variety of blocks, students now learn how block shapes relate to their functionality.

Students explore a variety of math reporters (from the Operators palette): four basic operations (+, −, ×, ÷), round, mod, and logical operators (and, or, and not) and they refine their understanding of the random block.

Technical tips:

  • People sometimes wonder about the use of compass directions rather than Cartesian directions. Here's some history:
    • This tradition started in Logo in the 1980s and was inherited by Scratch, then by snap.
    • In Logo, the turtle had the arrowhead shape that's our default, and pointed up by default. This is the right thing for fractal trees, and as good as anything for other turtle geometry exercises like the ones in BJC.
    • Scratch shifted the focus from turtle graphics to animation of characters, so sprites have costumes, such as their trademark cat, that move mostly horizontally. So they ended up with Logo's compass directions, but with a default direction of East (90 degrees).
    • The choice of direction measure in Snap! is motivated mostly by the desire to be compatible with Scratch, so that we have access to their 10 million uploaded projects.

  • Some vocabulary (for you to use in context, not to require of students):
    • A command is a single Lego-brick-shaped block, without its inputs.
    • A reporter is a single oval-shaped block, without its inputs.
    • A predicate is a single hexagonal block, without its inputs.
    • An instruction is a command block along with its input expressions.
    • An expression is a reporter block along with its input expressions.
    • A Boolean expression is a predicate block along with its input expressions.
    • A Boolean value is or . (Not the text strings "true" and "false"; not 0 and 1.)
    So a reporter or a predicate is a function; an expression is a function applied to arguments.
