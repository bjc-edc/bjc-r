Lab 1: Click Alonzo Game

Students set up their snap accounts so that they can save their projects and then dive right into programming, in the very first class, by building a game that they can then play on their smart phone and share with others. The object of the game is to click on Alonzo (the character at right) as he moves increasingly quickly around the screen.

This first lab is designed to hook students' interest in programming. Students will explore many of the ideas introduced here more formally later in this and other units. Students explore the snap interface and build a simple game app that they can run on their phones. In doing so, they learn to respond to user interaction (in this case, a click) and to use multiple cooperating scripts.

Pacing

The 4 required lab pages could be split across 2-3 days (55-110 minutes). Expected times to complete follow:

  • 10-20 minutes (<1 class period) on Getting Started with Snap!
    • If at all possible, do page 1 for your students before the first day of class so they can spend their entire first class period experiencing the beauty and joy of computing. Then try to fit pages 2-4 into the first day.
  • 15-30 minutes (<1 class period) on Programming Your App
  • 20-40 minutes (<1 class period) on Make It a Game
  • 10-20 minutes (<1 class period) on Sharing Your Game
  • If used, 10-20 minutes (<1 class period) on Improving Your Game*

Prepare

  • Create your own snap account. Instructions are on this student page.
  • We definitely recommend doing this lab yourself before class. Once you've set up your snap account, you can probably do the actual programming work of this lab in 25 minutes. (Later labs are more dense, and ask students to reflect on why they're doing what they're doing, but in this first lab the goal is for them to achieve a result that will excite and encourage them, without worrying about details.)
  • Students need an email account to create a snap account.
    • School email accounts are fine if they accept incoming mail from outside of the school network.
    • If it is possible for you to communicate with students before school starts, ask students to be sure they know their email address and password for the first day.
    • Better yet, suggest that they create Snap! accounts before the first class. You can point them at the lab page that explains how.
    • In case students have no email address or can't remember it, one solution is to use a free temporary email service — do a web search for "temporary email," but be sure your network doesn't block connections to the site you select.
    • Note for middle school teachers: If a student under age 13 creates an account, the dialog box asks for a parent's email rather than the student's own email. This is a legal requirement for snap to be compliant with the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act.

As a Class

  • So that students get the immediate excitement of building their own app, postpone introductory classroom management tasks (e.g., establishing classroom routines) until after Lab 1. Spend the entire first day programming. The goal is for them to finish the game on the first day.
  • Only after they have a successful programming experience behind them, introduce any additional educational technology (Google classroom, Piazza, etc.) that you'd like students to use.

Lab Pages

  • Page 1: Getting Started with Snap!
    • Learning Goal: Log in to Snap! via the Cloud menu, , using a personal username and password.
    • Tips:
      • A few students will have unpredictable problems creating snap accounts. To avoid letting these problems eat up the entire period, take advantage of pair programming; it's okay if one member of a pair doesn't have an account yet. You can work with the non-account students after class to get over that hurdle. (Even better, if you can wrangle a teaching assistant or two from last year's students, this would be a great thing for them to handle during the first class.)
      • The actual pair programming swap that comes partway through the project should be quick, but you'll have to devote a few minutes to discussing how pairs are supposed to work; specifically, the partner whose hands aren't on the keyboard should be actively contributing. Successful pairs are constantly talking.
  • Page 2: Programming Your App.
    • Learning Goals:
      • Create a response to a user interaction (in this case, a click) by using the when I am clicked hat block.
      • Learn to load and save projects in the cloud.
      • Begin the use of pair programming and to understand the value of this way of working.
    • Tips:
      • Some students may resist pair programming, arguing that they work better alone. Tell them to give pair programming an honest try during the first lab or two, and then you'll entertain ideas about modifying the way the pair works if they still want that.
      • When kids aren't cooperating and both have Snap! open, just go up to them and say: "so, who's the driver and who is the navigator?" and stay over their shoulder until one opens up the lab and yields.
      • Visit csteachingtips.org for more suggestions on pair programming.
  • Page 3: Make It a Game.
    • Learning Goal: Introduce repetition with the forever block.
    • Tips:
      • The reason we use the ghost effect to "keep score" is that we are trying to avoid introducing variables in this lab. The reason for that is to be able to start with local variables: procedure inputs and for block index variables. In Unit 2, students will use a variable to keep score formally.
      • There are many opportunities here to teach things, e.g., visible stepping to aid debugging. Resist the temptation! Your goal is to get to the end of this lab as quickly as possible, with every student successful. Visible stepping is taught in Lab 3 of this unit.
  • Page 4: Sharing Your Game.
    • Learning Goal: Share programs by email and to mobile devices.
    • Tip: Make sure both partners end up with a copy of the project in their accounts.
  • Page 5: Improving Your Game.
    • Learning Goal: Understand hat blocks and the beginning of multi-threading (more than one script can run at a time).
    • Tips:
      • A script doesn't have to have a hat block; you can run any script by clicking on it. Scripts without hat blocks are convenient when tinkering with a project. For example, you can make utility scripts just for the programmer, not for the end user of the project (e.g., put the sprite in the middle of the stage and clear the pen traces).
      • When using the say block, some students may expect the sprite to make a sound; instead this block makes the sprite display a speech cloud containing the message, as in a cartoon.

Correlation with AP CS Principles Framework 

Enduring Understandings:

Students write programs to execute algorithms (EU 5.2) that implement a sequence of instructions for the computer to follow (EU 4.1). This lab builds toward these EUs by focusing on a simple game algorithm in which students explore procedures that control sprite appearance and movement. This lab provides line-by-line support for project development as students familiarize themselves with the Snap! programming interface.

    Suggestions to consider:
    • EU 5.1 Programs can be developed for creative expression, to satisfy personal curiosity, to create new knowledge, or to solve problems (to help people, organizations, or society).
