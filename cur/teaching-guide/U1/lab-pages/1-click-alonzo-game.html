Lab 1: Click Alonzo Game

Students dive right into programming, in the very first class, by building a game that they can play and share with others. The object of the game is to click on Alonzo (the character at right) as he moves increasingly quickly around the screen. Optionally, they can put the game on their smartphone, which is great for motivation.

This first lab is designed to hook students' interest in programming. Students will explore many of the ideas introduced here more formally later. Here, students explore the Snap! interface and learn to respond to user interaction (in this case, a click).

Spend the entire first day with the students programming, without an introductory lecture. Since the goal is for all students to experience the immediate excitement of building a functioning game, postpone introductory classroom management tasks (e.g., establishing classroom routines) until after Lab 1. Do, however, make sure students understand that if they need help with something, or they're not sure they understand something, it's always okay to talk with their neighbors. Only after they have a successful programming experience behind them, introduce any additional educational technology (Google classroom, Piazza, etc.) that you'd like students to use.

Pacing

The 4 required lab pages could be split across 1-2 days (40-80 minutes). Expected times to complete follow:
  • 5-10 minutes (<1 class period) on Getting Started with Snap!
  • 15-30 minutes (<1 class period) on Programming a Game
  • 5-10 minutes (<1 class period) on Sharing Your Game
  • 15-30 minutes (<1 class period) on Giving the Player Feedback
  • If used, 10-20 minutes (<1 class period) on Improving Your Game*

Prepare

  • If you haven't already, create your own Snap! account and sign up for access to BJC solutions and assessments. Instructions for creating an account are on this student page.
  • Do this lab yourself before class. Once you've set up your snap account, you can probably do the actual programming work of this lab in 25 minutes or less. (Later labs are more dense and ask students to reflect on why they're doing what they're doing, but in this first lab, the goal is for them to achieve a result that will excite and encourage them, without worrying about details.)
  • Plan for students' Snap! use:
    • Students need an email account to create a snap account. School email accounts are fine if they accept incoming mail from outside of the school network. If it is possible for you to communicate with students before school starts, ask students to be sure they know their email address and password for the first day. Better yet, suggest that they create Snap! accounts before the first class. You can direct them to the first lab page, which explains how.
    • Note that if a student under age 13 creates an account, the dialog box asks for a parent's email rather than the student's own email. This is a legal requirement for snap to be compliant with the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act.
    • Some school districts will not allow your students to use Snap! accounts for coursework. If you are in one of your districts you can still use Snap!. Accounts aren't required to use Snap!, but they are extremely helpful. Without an account, students will need to "export" each of their projects every day, store the files themselves, and import them again when they want to continue their work. With an account, their projects are stored in the Snap! cloud and they can save and access them through the Snap! File menu.

Solutions

Correlation with 2020 AP CS Principles Framework 

Essential Knowledge:

  • CRD-2.B.1: A program is a collection of program statements that performs a specific task when run by a computer. A program is often referred to as software.
  • CRD-2.C.2: An event is associated with an action and supplies input data to a program.
  • CRD-2.C.3: Events can be generated when a key is pressed, a mouse is clicked, a program is started, or by any other defined action that affects the flow of execution.
  • CRD-2.C.5: In event-driven programming, program statements are executed when triggered rather than through the sequential flow of control.
