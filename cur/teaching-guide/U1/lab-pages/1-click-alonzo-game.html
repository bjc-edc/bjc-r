Lab 1: Click Alonzo Game

Students set up their snap accounts so that they can save their projects and then dive right into programming, in the very first class, by building a game that they can then play on their smart phone and share with others. The game is to click on Alonzo (at right) as he moves increasingly quickly around the screen.

This first lab is designed to hook students' interest in programming. Students will explore many of the ideas introduced here more formally later in this and other units. Students explore the snap interface and build a simple game app that they can run on their phones. In doing so, they learn to respond to user intervention (in this case, a click) and to compose blocks with one another (e.g., calling go to with inputs determined by pick random).

Pacing

These estimates are for the lab pages only. Additional time will be required for Computing the News or other activities.

The 7 lab pages could be split across 3-5 days (100-195 minutes). Expected times to complete follow:

  • 15-30 minutes (<1 class period) on Getting Started with Snap!
  • 20-40 minutes (<1 class period) on Programming Your App
  • 5 minutes (<1 class period) on Pair Programming Swap
  • 15-30 minutes (<1 class period) on Using the Random Block
  • 10-20 minutes (<1 class period) on Greeting the Player
  • 20-40 minutes (<1 class period) on Make It a Game
  • 15-30 minutes (<1 class period) on Sharing Your App

Prepare

  • Create your own snap account. Instructions are on this student page.
  • We definitely recommend doing this lab yourself before class. Once you've set up your snap account, you can probably do the actual programming work of this lab in 25 minutes. (Later labs are more dense, and ask students to reflect on why they're doing what they're doing, but in this first lab the goal is for them to achieve a result that will excite and encourage them, without worrying about details.)
  • Students need an email account to create a snap account.
    • School email accounts are fine if they accept incoming mail from outside of the school network.
    • If it is possible for you to communicate with students before school starts, ask students to be sure they know their email address and password for the first day.
    • Better yet, suggest that they create Snap! accounts before the first class. You can point them at the lab page that explains how.
    • In case students have no email address or can't remember it, one solution is to use a free temporary email service — do a web search for "temporary email," but be sure your network doesn't block connections to the site you select.
    • Note for middle school teachers: If a student under age 13 creates an account, the dialog box asks for a parent's email rather than the student's own email. This is a legal requirement for snap to be compliant with the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act.
  • The article How Pair Programming Really Works by Stuart Wray calls into question the "driver/navigator" metaphor everyone uses to explain pair programming with research showing a more complex social interaction between partners. The article is about professional programmers, not students, but many of its ideas are applicable in the classroom.
  • Prepare and copy a "Computer Use Agreement" (see this example) for students to sign to hold them accountable for using computers responsibly.
  • Prepare several Computing in the News stories.

As a Class

  • So that students get the immediate excitement of building their own app, consider postponing as much introductory material (e.g., establishing classroom routines) as possible until after Lab 1. Try to preserve at least half of the first lesson to let them get started building the game, working in pairs, before taking care of other first-day business.
  • Only after they have a successful programming experience behind them, introduce any additional educational technology (Google classroom, Piazza, etc.) that you'd like students to use.
  • Be sure students understand that are expected to read all the material presented and complete all the "For You To Do" problems with only the following exceptions:
    • Yellow boxes are optional reading.
    • "If There Is Time..." and "Take It Further" problems are not required. (These are provided for students to work on after finishing the required reading and problems. "If There Is Time..." problems are generally about as difficult as "For You To Do" problems, but "Take It Further" problems are often harder. If pairs have finished their assigned work, they can go ahead and start on some of these extra problems and don't have to wait for permission.)
    • Gray boxes contain AP vocabulary. These can be skipped the first time around, but should be read during exam preparation.

Lab Pages

In this first lab, some of the goals are purely administrative rather than computer science goals. In later labs, goals will be more focused on computer science.

  • Page 1: Getting Started with Snap!
    • Learning Goal: Log in to Snap! via the Cloud menu, , using a personal username and password.
    • Tips:
      • A few students will have unpredictable problems creating snap accounts. To avoid letting these problems eat up the entire period, take advantage of pair programming; it's okay if one member of a pair doesn't have an account yet. You can work with the non-account students after class to get over that hurdle. (Even better, if you can wrangle a teaching assistant or two from last year's students, this would be a great thing for them to handle during the first class.)
      • The actual pair programming swap that comes partway through the project should be quick, but you'll have to devote a few minutes to discussing how pairs are supposed to work; specifically, the partner whose hands aren't on the keyboard should be actively contributing. Successful pairs are constantly talking.
  • Page 2: Programming Your App.
    • Learning Goal: Create a response to a user intervention (in this case, a click) by using the when I am clicked hat block.
  • Page 3: Pair Programming Swap.
    • Learning Goal: Begin the use of pair programming and to understand the value of this way of working.
    • Tip: Some students will strongly resist pair programming, arguing that they work better alone. Tell them to give pair programming an honest try during the first lab or two, and then you'll entertain ideas about modifying the way the pair works if they still want that.
  • Page 4: Using the Random Block.
    • Learning Goals:
      • Compose one block with another, e.g., making a call to the go to block with inputs that are determined by the the pick random operation.
      • Learn to load and save projects in the cloud.
      • Get to know the Snap! coordinate system.
    • Mathematics Note: The coordinate axes may be a challenge for some students. In the spirit of this first lab, it's not so important that students understand the Snap! coordinate axes now, and if they don't, just have them follow the instructions literally: a random number between -190 and 190 for x, a random number between -130 and 130 for y. For your reference, here (at right) is the underlying 'anatomy' of the Snap! stage; this background image is available in Snap! by clicking the Stage icon and selecting "Backgrounds" from the File menu, then "xy-grid."
  • Page 5: Greeting the Player.
    • Learning Goal: Understand hat blocks and the beginning of multithreading (more than one script can run at a time).
    • Tips:
      • A script doesn't have to have a hat block; you can run any script by clicking on it. This is convenient when tinkering with a project; you can have utility scripts that, e.g., put the sprite in the middle of the stage and clear the pen traces.
      • When using the say block, some students may expect the sprite to make a sound; instead this block makes the sprite display a speech cloud containing the message, as in a cartoon.)
  • Page 6: Make It a Game.
    • Learning Goal: Introduce repetition with the forever block.
    • Tips:
      • The reason we use the ghost effect to "keep score" is that we are trying to avoid introducing variables in this lab. The reason for that is to be able to start with local variables: procedure inputs and for block index variables.
      • There are many opportunities here to teach things, e.g., visible stepping to aid debugging. Resist the temptation! Your goal is to get to the end of this lab as quickly as possible, with every student successful.
  • Page 7: Sharing Your App.
    • Learning Goal: Share programs by email and to mobile devices.
    • Tip: Make sure both partners end up with a copy of the project in their accounts.

Correlation with AP CS Principles Framework 

Enduring Understandings:

Students write programs to execute algorithms (EU 5.2) that implement a sequence of instructions for the computer to follow (EU 4.1). This lab builds toward these EUs by focusing on a simple game algorithm in which students explore procedures that control sprite appearance and movement. This lab provides line-by-line support for project development as students familiarize themselves with the Snap! programming interface.

    Suggestions to consider:
    • EU 5.1 Programs can be developed for creative expression, to satisfy personal curiosity, to create new knowledge, or to solve problems (to help people, organizations, or society).

Learning Objectives:

Students collaborate to develop a program in Snap! (LO 4.1.2) to execute algorithms that control sprite appearance and movement (LO 5.1.3) in order to implement the desired behavior in a simple game (LO 4.1.1).

    • LO 5.1.3 Collaborate to develop a program. [P6]
