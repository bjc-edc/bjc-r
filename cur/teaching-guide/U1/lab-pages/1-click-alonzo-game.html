  • Page 1: Getting Started with Snap!
    • Learning Goal: Log in to Snap! via the Cloud menu, , using a personal username and password.
    • Tips:
      • A few students will have unpredictable problems creating snap accounts. To avoid letting these problems eat up the entire period, take advantage of pair programming; it's okay if one member of a pair doesn't have an account yet. You can work with the non-account students after class to get over that hurdle. (Even better, if you can wrangle a teaching assistant or two from last year's students, this would be a great thing for them to handle during the first class.)
  • Page 2: Programming a Game.
    • Learning Goals:
      • Create a response to a user interaction (in this case, a click) by using the when I am clicked hat block.
      • Introduce repetition with the forever block.
      • Learn to log in and save projects in the cloud.
    • Tips:
      • This lab is not the place to talk about or enforce pair programming. Suggest that if they pair up, one partner can have the lab page onscreen while the other has Snap! onscreen, but don't make a fuss about it. That comes in Lab 2. For now, the goal is to get through the entire lab on the first day.
        • In fact, you might want students to work individually on the first day, to ensure that they all know how to manipulate the Snap! environment.
      • Each chunk of work starts with a sentence or two explaining why they're doing that chunk, e.g., "You're going to program a game in which the goal is to click on Alonzo as he's moving around."
      • This page, like the rest of the course, is meant for students to read and do on their own. But on the first day of the class, too much of the difficulty is about the mechanics of the user interface, rather than about the CS ideas. So, for this page only, if things seem to be going too slowly, consider having the class follow along with you as you demonstrate what to do, step by step, at the projector.
        • Don't worry if the block changes color from to when you insert it in your script. Snap! alternates dark and light colors when you nest same-palette blocks inside each other.
      • After For You To Do 6, we expect that the student will have built this script:

        Even if you consider the first go to block redundant, don't tell students to remove it! Later in the project, additional code will make it important to keep the two separate.
      • In the starter file, the Alonzo sprite's "draggable" checkbox (just under its name, above the scripting area) is unchecked. This isn't the default, so if your students want to build a similar project on their own, tell them to uncheck it. This is important when a project uses "when I am clicked," because it's easy to move your hand on the mouse slightly while pushing the button, and the computer might treat that as a short dragging rather than a clicking. Unchecking "draggable" rules out dragging.
  • Page 3: Sharing Your Game.
    • Learning Goal: Share programs by email and to mobile devices.
    • Tips:
      • Make sure both partners end up with a copy of the project in their accounts.
      • Students should share their projects, but not publish them. Sharing lets them give the project URL to friends, but publishing also displays the project on the Snap! web site. It's not a big problem if they do that, but it means that 1000 copies of the same assignment crowd out other projects.
      • Page 2 created the minimum playable game, and page 3 is saving and sharing it. Bear in mind, and tell students, that when they improve the game on page 4, those improvements will automatically be sent to their friends and their phone as soon as they save to the cloud. That's why we interrupt the programming (pages 2, 4, and 5) with this sharing page; we are trying to make sure they have something to show their friends and their parents the first day of class! You may finish page 4 also on the first day, if your kids are efficient and you remember not to lecture, but if not, they still have something to show today, and something even better after the second class meeting.
        • Why are your friends' copies updated automatically when you improve the project, but your partner's copy isn't? This may feel strange to students. The answer is that your friends don't really have a copy at all; what they have is a link that allows them to read your own copy. A link is all they need, because they're just playing the game, not programming it. But your partner needs a copy because they need to be able to edit it without you, if you miss a class. Your two copies are two separate projects, as far as Snap! is concerned. This is something you (the teacher) can say to a student who asks, but don't make links vs. copies an issue for the class. Some other week, maybe, but for now, don't let anything slow down the class.
  • Page 4: Keeping Score.
    • Learning Goals:
      • Understand the difference between sprites and costumes.
      • Get aquainted with conditionals.
      • Experience playtuning a game.
    • Tips:
      • The reason we use the ghost effect to "keep score" is that we are trying to avoid introducing variables in this lab. The reason for that is to be able to start with local variables: procedure inputs and for block index variables. In Unit 2, students will use a variable to keep score formally.
      • There are many opportunities here to teach things, e.g., visible stepping to aid debugging. Resist the temptation! Your goal is to get to the end of this lab as quickly as possible, with every student successful. Visible stepping is taught in Lab 3 of this unit.
      • This page introduces an IF statement, with no explanation. That's deliberate. Some students will just do as they're told and not worry about what it means; some will think about it for a moment and find it perfectly clear; some may find it confusing. Encourage those latter students to keep going, play the game, and see if it starts making sense to them. Assure them that everything in this lab will be taught "properly" a little later.
  • Page 5: Improving Your Game.
    • Learning Goal: Understand hat blocks and the beginning of multi-threading (more than one script can run at a time).
    • Tips:
      • A script doesn't have to have a hat block; you can run any script by clicking on it. Scripts without hat blocks are convenient when tinkering with a project. For example, you can make utility scripts just for the programmer, not for the end user of the project (e.g., put the sprite in the middle of the stage and clear the pen traces).
      • When using the say block, some students may expect the sprite to make a sound; instead this block makes the sprite display a speech cloud containing the message, as in a cartoon.

Correlation with 2020 AP CS Principles Framework 

Essential Knowledge:

  • CRD-2.B.1: A program is a collection of program statements that performs a specific task when run by a computer. A program is often referred to as software.
  • CRD-2.C.2: An event is associated with an action and supplies input data to a program.
  • CRD-2.C.3: Events can be generated when a key is pressed, a mouse is clicked, a program is started, or by any other defined action that affects the flow of execution.
  • CRD-2.C.5: In event-driven programming, program statements are executed when triggered rather than through the sequential flow of control.
