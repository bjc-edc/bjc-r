    • Start by writing

    • Use techniques from Danielson’s “accountable talk”
    • Make sure they cite what they’re saying
    • Encourage them to respect what each other is saying

Students begin to see that there are many social implications to computing. The Blown to Bits reading highlights ways the world has been changed by computing and identifies examples that might not look at first like a "bits story" but is. In this lab, students get a range of experiences, including reading the book, beginning to compile a list of computing innovations, debating the hot topic of whether violent video games cause youth violence, and finding evidence for ways that technology can be both good and bad.

Activities related to finding and describing computing innovations helps prepare students for the Explore Performance Task. The writing and video exercises (such as making a video about an innovation, supporting a claim about privacy using evidence, supporting a claim about video games using evidence, and brainstorming evidence for claims about whether technology is good or bad) also prepare students for the Explore Task. Important AP topics that are addressed in this Lab include a preliminary exploration of bits and looking into a broad range of computing innovations.

Guiding Question: What does Tanya Rider's story have to do with the explosion of bits?

Much of Blown to Bits has to do with the balance between the abundance of digital information and our attempts for retaining privacy and ownership of our own information. Have students read the selection with the guiding question in mind, then discuss briefly. Students may also be interested in looking up news stories or videos about Tanya Rider. Examples:

Looking ahead
Next week students will form groups of 2-3, choose a computing innovation that interests them, and produce a short video that explains the innovation and some of its impacts. Eventually, students will use this experience to help them with their AP CS Principles Explore Task, about implications of computing innovations.

Look through these lists of recent innovations:

  • Wharton's top 30 innovations
  • CNN's 10 ideas
Not all of these are computing innovations, but many are related to computing.

Guiding Question: Which of these innovations are you familiar with? Look up a few innovations that you are unfamiliar with to see what they do, what they are used for, and what kind of impact they have on the way we live.

Note: You might encourage students to start their own Google doc to keep track of their findings. As they look up information online, encourage them to be in the habit of keeping track of and citing their sources.
