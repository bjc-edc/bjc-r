Lab 5: Censorship and Computing around the World

This lab takes the broad structure and content of Blown to Bits Chapter 7. Students first consider what rules (if any) ought to exist on the Internet and then learn of some of the practical difficulties of regulating Internet content. This is also an opportunity for students to think further about their own role as digital citizens. Students then consider some issues of free speech and are confronted with the fact that a global Internet leads to issues when different countries have different standards for what should be censored. Finally, students take this opportunity to examine some statistics for Internet use around the world.

Beware that many potential sensitive topics are addressed on these pages.

  • The first story in the Blown to Bits reading is about a romantic connection online. While dramatic, this particular story did not have a devastating end, but it is possible that students may have experienced stalking or worse online.
  • Chapter 7 in Blown to Bits examines censorship primarily in terms of how pornographic materials are treated online. The students are not assigned reading from most of these sections, but students may read these sections and have questions or raise issues.
  • In materials about Internet censorship, specific countries are identified as having particularly harsh censorship policies. The United States is not immune from this, but be aware of statements that are made that appear to judge people of other nationalities. Part of the point of thinking through the social implications of these issues is to realize that these issues are not simple, and that different policies reflect different ways that societies have grappled with difficult and complex issues.
  • Finally, students will discuss the "digital divide" at the end of this Lab. Be aware of students who themselves do not have a computer at home. There are policies and innovations that are being developed to eliminate this digital divide, and it is an issue that all students can work to address.

Pacing:

There are four pages in this lab. The 4 lab pages could be split across 3-4 days (150-200 minutes). Expected times to complete follow:
  • 30-40 minutes on Rules of the Internet
  • 35-45 minutes on Regulating the Internet
  • 40-50 minutes on Censorship
  • 35-45 minutes on Computing Around the World

Prepare:

  • Computing in the News stories this week might focus on stories about computing from different parts of the world.
  • Students will read parts of Chapter 7 of Blown to Bits. The chapter goes in depth into the challenges of regulating the Internet. It explores different metaphors for the Internet and the ways that the government and courts have tried to use these metaphors in their decisions. These metaphors may provide interesting ways for you to get your students to think about the Internet. The chapter itself, as mentioned above, focuses much of its discussion on the regulation of pornographic content. This is because of our country's specific history and how it has dealt with First Amendment rights. Even without this focus, however, students can still consider issues of censorship, especially in ways that, for example, they might experience limits on what they are able to access in schools.

Goals for the Lab:

  • Page 1: Rules of the Internet.
    • Understand some of the risks implied by an Internet without rule enforcement with respect to speech.
  • Page 2: Regulating the Internet.
    • Understand some of the complications involved in enforcing rules on the Internet.
  • Page 3: Censorship.
    • Consider their own views on censorship and learn about Internet censorship around the world.
    • Understand the impacts of bullying and the impacts of restricting speech.
  • Page 4: Computing Around the World.
    • Understand the "digital divide" and some of the broad political implications.

As a Class:

  • Page 1: Rules of the Internet.
    • Have students brainstorm, in partners first, and then with a class if you wish, ways that people can be harmed by what is put on the Internet. Examples could include cyberbullying messages, defamation (libel), personal data that gets revealed, incorrect medical advice, or lies meant to trick someone, to name a few.
    • The questions under problem 2 are meant to inspire some debate. There are several ways to do this.
      • You could have a whole class discussion.
      • You could have 3 posters up in the room (or these can be 3 sections of a whiteboard, for example). Each poster addresses one of the questions. The first poster has two halves: "Yes, people should be allowed to say anything online" or "No, people should not be allowed to say anything they want online." The second poster has three sections: "The person posting the information should be held responsible" "The owner of the webpage should be held responsible" "No one should be held responsible." The third poster has two sections: "Yes they should be protected. Here's how" and "No, here's why." Then, give each student at least 3 sticky notes for them to write reasons for their opinion, and have them post these in the appropriate section. These can then serve as fodder for a whole-class discussion.
      • You might choose to just deal with one of these questions (or allow students to choose one question to tackle). Students can come up with a short "soapbox" speech, a 2-minute speech where they defend their position on one of these questions.
      • Instead of talking about reasons for supporting certain positions, you can focus the entire discussion on why these questions are complex. Why is it a complex issue to figure out if people should be allowed to say anything on the Internet? Reasons include free speech issues, the fact that it's possible to post anonymously, the fact that real people can be hurt by information online, the fact that people have different standards for what is appropriate or not, etc.
  • Page 2: Regulating the Internet.
    • The Blown to Bits reading is about a case that led to DOPA, which never actually became law. Students will probably relate to this law because they likely have experienced being blocked from certain web content in schools. Students are likely familiar with the idea that blocking content necessarily blocks not just harmful material, but also harmless and potentially useful information as well. Students also know that just because they cannot access certain content in schools does not mean that they are protected from this content completely.
    • The questions in problem 2 are meant to be guiding questions that students can use to make sure they understand what they are reading. A short whole-class discussion about these questions can be used to check that people understand the role of ISPs as described in the reading, and the complexities in assigning responsibility and regulating content.
    • The "Take It Further" reading is a short, interesting read that highlights that harm can come to completely innocent victims. Does this change how students think about whether people should be held responsible for their actions in spreading misinformation?
  • Page 3: Censorship.
    • Students have now thought about these issues a bit more. In this Four Corners activity, have students go to their Four Corners according to their views, but also ask students whether anyone has changed their minds about these issues over the last few days.
    • As part of the Four Corners activity, you can also give the opportunity for certain viewpoints to try to persuade people in the other groups to come to their corners.
    • The writing activity is an opportunity for students to construct a well-organized paragraph stating their view and evidence to support this view.
    • The second set of questions starts with a Blown to Bits reading about policies in different countries and how these policies interact with people and businesses in the U.S. The main point of this section is in question 5 which asks students to discuss or write about why they should care about what happens in other countries.
    • In the "Take It Further" problems, students can explore world maps at opennet.net, and read about the Arab Spring, which was famously associated with the power of social media.
  • Page 4: Computing Around the World.
    • Students will look at some country level statistics about Internet usage.
    • Discuss as a class what the different column headings measure, and why each column is useful in getting a more complete picture of who in the world has access to the Internet.
    • Spend some time with the third bullet point of problem 1, with students coming up with questions they have about Internet usage. You might even consider have students do some extra online research into some of their questions. Look for a variety of questions, such as:
      • When was the Internet first introduced into these countries?
      • What percent of people have a computer?
      • Just because people have access to the Internet, does that mean they use it?
      • How did these countries get to where they are? Are there different government policies? What are the differences in infrastructure?
      • How much do people in different countries pay for Internet? Does the price vary a lot from country to country?
      • and so on.
    • Students can have a discussion or write about the digital divide. Be aware that some students do not have a computer or Internet access at home. The digital divide can create an unfair society, but there are ways of addressing this divide, such as providing free Internet and advocating for more access to technology in schools. Also consider how more technology is not strictly positive. What are ways that our society might be affected negatively by the prevalence of technology?
    • The "Take It Further" prompts offer some other perspectives about computing in the U.S. and around the world.

Correlation with AP CS Principles Framework:

Enduring Understandings:

  • EU 7.1 Computing enhances communication, interaction, and cognition.
  • EU 7.3 Computing has a global effect — both beneficial and harmful — on people and society.
  • EU 7.4 Computing innovations influence and are influenced by the economic, social, and cultural contexts in which they are designed and used.

Learning Objectives:

  • LO 7.1.1 Explain how computing innovations affect communication, interaction, and cognition. [P4]
  • LO 7.3.1 Analyze the beneficial and harmful effects of computing. [P4]
  • LO 7.4.1 Explain the connections between computing and economic, social, and cultural contexts. [P1]

Essential Knowledge:

  • EK 7.1.1C Social media continues to evolve and fosters new ways to communicate.
  • EK 7.1.1E Widespread access to information facilitates the identification of problems, development of solutions, and dissemination of results.
  • EK 7.1.1N The Internet and the Web have changed many areas, including e-commerce, health care, access to information and entertainment, and online learning.
  • EK 7.3.1C Access to digital content via peer-to-peer networks raises legal and ethical concerns.
  • EK 7.3.1D Both authenticated and anonymous access to digital information raise legal and ethical concerns.
  • EK 7.3.1E Commercial and governmental censorship of digital information raise legal and ethical concerns.
  • EK 7.3.1G Privacy and security concerns arise in the development and use of computational systems and artifacts.
  • EK 7.3.1H Aggregation of information, such as geolocation, cookies, and browsing history, raises privacy and security concerns.
  • EK 7.3.1I Anonymity in online interactions can be enabled through the use of online anonymity software and proxy servers.
  • EK 7.3.1J Technology enables the collection, use, and exploitation of information about, by, and for individuals, groups, and institutions.
  • EK 7.4.1A The innovation and impact of social media and online access varies in different countries and in different socioeconomic groups.
  • EK 7.4.1B Mobile, wireless, and networked computing have an impact on innovation throughout the world.
  • EK 7.4.1C The global distribution of computing resources raises issues of equity, access, and power.
  • EK 7.4.1D Groups and individuals are affected by the "digital divide" – differing access to computing and the Internet based on socioeconomic or geographic characteristics.
  • EK 7.4.1E Networks and infrastructure are supported by both commercial and governmental initiatives.
