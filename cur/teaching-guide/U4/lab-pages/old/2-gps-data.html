Programming Lab 2: GPS Data

In this lab, students use Snap! to model and process a batch of GPS coordinates representing the locations of potential clients for a new restaurant in New York City. Building on the work on reading data from Lab 1, students create an appropriately scaled model of the data and analyze it to determine the optimal location for the new business.

Pacing:

The 3 lab pages could be split across 3 or 4 days (150-190 minutes). Expected times to complete follow:
  • 30-40 minutes (<1 class period) on GPS Coordinates
  • 60-75 minutes (1-2 class periods) on Interpreting and Displaying GPS Coordinates
  • 60-75 minutes (1-2 class periods) on Analyzing GPS Data

Prepare:

  • Computing in the News stories during this lab might focus on stories about GPS, location tracking services, and issues around sharing personally-identifying information.

Students will:

Looking Ahead
  • In Unit 5, students will do further work with modeling and analyzing data.
  • Learn about GPS coordinates.
  • Import formatted data into Snap!
  • Use the stamp block to visualize the set of GPS coordinates.
  • Scale data for effective viewing, and draw conclusions from a visualization.
  • Build a process for selecting a relevant subset of data from the larger set.
  • Split data into clusters and interpret the data.

Goals for the Lab:

  • Page 1: GPS Coordinates.
    • See a list of GPS coordinates as a list of locations.
  • Page 2: Interpreting and Displaying GPS Coordinates.
    • Understand how latitude and longitude values specify physical locations on Earth.
    • Understand how GPS coordinates specify latitude and longitude values.
    • Understand how coordinate values can be translated and dilated by adding and multiplying the x and y values.
  • Page 3: Analyzing GPS Data.
    • Visualize GPS coordinates on a map.
    • Filter data based on characteristics.
    • Perform cluster analysis, taking a set of data and breaking it into clusters based on characteristics of the data for further analysis.
    • Use a model to inform decision-making, and recognize the assumptions included in the model and the implications those assumptions have for the appropriateness of the decision.

As a Class:

  • Page 1: GPS Coordinates.
    • Discussion: What is GPS?
      • Discuss the contents of the CSV file and the format of the data once it is read into Snap!.
      • How is GPS information reported? Where does it come from? Find out if students have intuition about GPS.
      • Do not address the details of latitude and longitude on this page; this topic is addressed on the following page.
  • Page 2: Interpreting and Displaying GPS Coordinates.
    • Discussion: What are latitude and longitude?
      • What does it mean when a GPS coordinate has a positive latitude? negative latitude? positive longitude? negative longitude?
      • What changes would you see for a point on a map when its latitude changes? when its longitude changes?
    • Discussion: Why is data scaling necessary in visualization?
      • What happens to points when you add or subtract a value from coordinates?
      • What happens to points when you multiply values by a scale factor? What happens if the scale factor is too big? too small? What scale factor is appropriate for this data?
    • Discussion: What information does the data visualization give you?
  • Page 3: Analyzing GPS Data.
    • Discussion: Why is it helpful to split data into subsets?
      • What happens when you take the average of all the GPS coordinates, relative to where the customers live?
      • What else could be tried instead?
      • What are some ways to split data into subsets?
      • What new information can you learn from the subset of data?

Assignments:

  • Students will develop two pieces of work in this lab: a project to visualize the GPS data at a scale where the shape of the data can be seen, and a project to identify and extract subsets (clusters) of the full data.
  • Assessment Idea:
    • Treasure Hunt! The set of GPS coordinates in this XML Snap! project file has been translated (a shift has been performed, but no dilation) so that they are distributed around the origin (0,0). The first point is actually the coordinates of the Boston Public Library in Boston, MA. Translate the points back so that they are distributed correctly. Then find the average latitude and longitude of the points. The corresponding location is the location of the treasure. What is the street address of the treasure?
      • Solution: (42.3395575, -71.09336503) = MFA, Boston, 465 Huntington Ave, Boston, MA 02115.

Tips:

  • Page 1: GPS Coordinates.
    • Do not address the details of latitude and longitude on this page; this topic is addressed on the following page.
    • It may help to discuss smartphones, which have GPS tracking, and briefly mention the network of satellites that provide the information.
    • GPS signals are not part of any cellular phone network, and there are many devices with GPS that are not connected to the Internet.
  • Page 2: Interpreting and Displaying GPS Coordinates.
    • When discussing longitude and latitude, be especially mindful of students reversing the order. Matching the words "longitude" and "long" can help, especially if you emphasize with your hands the left-right direction when saying "long".
    • Watch for students making errors when asked to subtract or add from each latitude and longitude, both in reversing the order and in reversing the operation. Correct plots here will center at (0,0) on the stage. If students ask why this step is needed, note that the centering to (0,0) allows the next step (scaling the data) to be done more simply.
    • Some students may need a reminder about the clear block to reset the stage.
    • There is more than one possible answer for the scale factor of data, typically any number between 200 and 1000 is good. Watch for students testing a series of very low values for the scale factor (2, 3, 4...), perhaps suggesting they jump to 10 or 100. At 100, some data separation starts to appear, and they should be fine from then. Less often, students may use an excessively large scale factor that pushes all the data off the stage completely; encourage them to find a value that makes the data visible, and they should be fine from then.
  • Page 3: Analyzing GPS Data.
    • Watch for students calculating the average GPS coordinates, then just plotting the average on the stage, instead of performing the data transformations. It may be helpful to have students use list operations to "add" the average as new coordinates in the original GPS data set temporarily, since the transformation code has already been written. If doing this, have students remove this before doing the clustering work.
    • If students are having trouble understanding the result of the averaging calculation, have them plot the location using a mapping service, then compare the location to the data presented in the map on this page. This should offer a more realized visualization than the one available in Snap!.
    • The description for the zone block is deliberately vague about how students declare the output. Most will probably choose numerical output (1, 2, 3). Look for students choosing text output ("Brooklyn" or "bottom") and highlight this option to the rest of the class.
    • You could ask students how they might use map to create a single list containing latitude, longitude, and zone. This task is not necessary to complete the work in this lab, but is an interesting application of list operations.
