Programming Lab 3: Network Protocols

This lab covers hexadecimal representation and the abstractions in the Internet (especially TCP/IP). The first page addresses hex (base 16) building on students' understanding of binary from Unit 1 to support understanding of RGB colors used in HTML/CSS and IPv6 addresses. The remaining pages cover the Internet protocol suite, including IP Address Hierarchy, IPv4 vs. IPv6, packets and packet switching, reliable data transmission, open standards, and the internet abstraction hierarchy. Students identify the IP address of the computer they are using and use Snap! to scrape the results of a "my ip address" query to a Server engine to make a my IP address block.

Pacing:

The 4 lab pages could be split across 3 or 4 days (135-173 minutes). Expected times to complete follow:
  • 45-55 minutes (1 class period) on Hexadecimal Representation
  • 30-40 minutes (<1 class period) on The Network of Networks: IP
  • 45-55 minutes (1 class period) on Reliable Transmission on Unreliable Networks: TCP
  • 15-25 minutes (<1 class period) on Open Protocols and Internet Abstraction Hierarchy

Prepare:

  • Computing in the News stories during this lab might address Net Neutrality. The FCC recently set new rules that (1) regulate broadband Internet providers like a public utility, and (2) prevent ISPs from charging certain companies (like YouTube) from priority delivery, and (3) will be challenged in courts.   • In Lab 5, students will look at the vulnerabilities of the Internet and the security measures designed to address them.
  • Extend their understanding of different number bases to include hexadecimal.
  • Explore hexadecimal representation and it's use in RGB color and IP addresses.

As a Class:

  • Page 1: Hexadecimal Representation.
    • Encourage students to take turns reading on this page.
    • Binary and hexadecimal representation can be a difficult topic for students, but mastery is not required here. The goal is for students to become familiar with the basics of hex. Consider translating between the representations for several numbers together at the board.
  • Page 2: The Network of Networks: IP.
    • Discussion: These discussion questions address the reading from Blown to Bits listed on the lab page and described in the "Assignments" section later on this page:
      • What is a circuit-switched network? What is a packet-switched network? (This is addressed on page 302 of Blown to Bits.)
      • What is an ISP? Who is your ISP? (Some students may know their Internet Service Provider, and those who do not may recognize the name of theirs upon hearing responses from others. For example, here are some ISPs in NYC. This topic is addressed on page 303 of Blown to Bits.)
    • Discussion: Generate a list of key ideas of the Internet—that is, key features that would need to be there (or questions to be addressed) in any sort of re-design of the internet. Use these prompts to stimulate the discussion:
      • Where does content on the internet live?
      • What happens if information gets corrupted as it travels?
      • How can you make sure that new technology can be developed and added to the Internet even if older systems are still in place?
      • How can you make sure that the huge files of the future are able to travel through the Internet built today?
  • Page 3: Reliable Transmission on Unreliable Networks: TCP.
    • Discussion: These discussion questions address the reading from Blown to Bits listed on the lab page and described in the "Assignments" section later on this page:
      • What is a protocol? What does it do for the Internet? What are some examples?
      • What is redundancy? How does it make the Internet more reliable?
    • Discussion: These discussion questions address the code.org video "The Internet: Packets, Routing, and Reliability" included on the lab page:
      • What does a packet contain? How does a packet-switched system work? (This is also addressed on page 302 of Blown to Bits.)
    • Discussion: This lab page includes a simulation of unreliable data transmission by Internet Protocol, and the last "For You To Do" item asks students to "Brainstorm what you could do to get around network unreliability." After students have had a chance to explore the simulation and consider this question, ask them to share their ideas. TCP sends meta-data along with each packet so that the receiving computer can reorder the packets and identify if there are any missing. How might this be accomplished in Snap! ?
  • Page 4: Open Protocols and Internet Abstraction Hierarchy.
    • Encourage students to take turns reading on this page.
    • Discussion: Consider having students describe each of the Internet Abstraction Hierarchy layers: Application, Transport, Internet, and Hardware.

Assignments:

Two pages in this lab start out with a brief (4-6 page) reading from the Blown to Bits appendix. Ideally, these assignments will be read before the class period that covers these lab pages:

  • Page 2: The Network of Networks: IP.
    • Read Blown to Bits pages 301-306.
    • You may wish to assign a brief writing assignment to help students solidify their understanding of address hierarchy. Have students describe what it means that:
      • Street addresses are hierarchical.
      • Domain names are hierarchical.
      • IP addresses are hierarchical.
  • Page 3: Reliable Transmission on Unreliable Networks: TCP.
    • Read Blown to Bits pages 306-309.

Tips:

  • Page 1: Hexadecimal Representation.
    • There are several number representation converter games included on this page. These are included to engage students in and to provide practice with changing number bases. You may wish to assign these as homework, or to use them on a shorter class period or on a day before vacation. In the classroom, watch that they do not distract students from the essential content of the unit.
  • Page 3: Reliable Transmission on Unreliable Networks: TCP.
    • The simulation included in this page only includes a demonstration of unreliable packet transmission for students to consider how they might address. More advanced students can attempt to develop their own version of TCP in the Take It Further section at the bottom of the page.

Essential Knowledge:

  • EK 6.1.1B An end-to-end architectures facilitates connecting new devices and networks on the Internet.
  • EK 6.1.1C Devices and networks that make up the Internet are connected and communicate using addresses and protocols.
  • EK 6.1.1D The Internet and the systems built on it facilitate collaboration.
  • EK 6.1.1E Connecting new devices to the Internet is enabled by assignment of an Internet protocol (IP) address.
  • EK 6.1.1F The Internet is built on evolving standards, including those for addresses and names.
  • EK 6.1.1G The domain name system (DNS) translates names to IP addresses.
  • EK 6.1.1H The number of devices that could use an IP address has grown so fast that a new protocol (IPv6) has been established to handle routing of many more devices.
  • EK 6.2.1A The Internet and the systems built on it are hierarchical and redundant.
  • EK 6.2.1C IP addresses are hierarchical.
  • EK 6.2.1D Routing on the Internet is fault tolerant and redundant.
  • EK 6.2.2A Hierarchy and redundancy help systems scale.
  • EK 6.2.2B The redundancy of routing (i.e., more than one way to route data) between two points on the Internet increases the reliability of the Internet and helps it scale to more devices and more people.
  • EK 6.2.2D Interfaces and protocols enable widespread use of the Internet.
  • EK 6.2.2E Open standards fuel the growth of the Internet.
  • EK 6.2.2F The Internet is a packet-switched system through which digital data is sent by breaking the data into blocks of bits called packets, which contain both the data being transmitted and control information for routing the data.
  • EK 6.2.2G Standards for packets and routing include transmission control protocol/Internet protocol (TCP/IP).
  • EK 6.2.2H Standards for sharing information and communicating between browsers and servers on the Web include HTTP and secure sockets layer/transport layer security (SSL/TLS).
  • EK 6.2.2I The size and speed of systems affect their use.
  • EK 6.2.2J The bandwidth of a system is a measure of bit rate – the amount of data (measured in bits) that can be sent in a fixed amount of time.
  • EK 6.2.2K The latency of a system is the time elapsed between the transmission and the receipt of a request.
