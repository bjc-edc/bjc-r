Lab 3: Cybersecurity

Students learn about various cybersecurity risks on the Internet (including the fundamental lack of DNS security and common security attacks) and the basic concepts of cryptography.

Pacing:

The 4 required lab pages could be split across 4-6 days (180-240 minutes). Expected times to complete follow:
  • 45-55 minutes (1 class period) on Cryptography
  • 30-40 minutes (< 1 class period) on Symmetric Cryptography
  • If used, 90-120 minutes (2-3 class periods) on Caesar Cipher Project*
  • 60-90 minutes (1-2 class periods) on Public Key Encryption
  • 45-55 minutes (1 class period) on Cybersecurity
  • If used, 65-75 minutes (1-2 class periods) on Who Cares About Encryption?*

Prepare:

  • As you do Computing in the News with your students this week, look for news stories relating to issues of encryption. Highlight stories that might give students ideas for their research task at the end of the lab about innovations that need to transmit data securely.
  • Students will read parts of Chapter 5 of Blown to Bits. Reading through the entire chapter will be helpful, especially to get a better understanding of the evolution of encryption methods and some details of public key encryption.

  • Page 1: Cryptography.
    • Learning Goals:
      • Understand what it means to encode and decode text.
      • Develop strategies for encoding and decoding a message.
      • Catch a glimpse into what public key encryption is and how it differs from private key encryption. (It is not necessary for students to understand the mathematics involved.)
      • Understand the difference between symmetric and asymmetric cryptography.
    • Tips:
      • Most students will greatly enjoy encoding and decoding secret messages. Make sure that there is enough time planned in the lesson for this hands-on activity.
      • It may be a good idea to suggest that for starters students try to encode/decode very short messages.
      • Some students may get frustrated in the decoding process, not being able to crack their partners code, even for short messages. Tell them that the challenging nature of decoding is to be expected and is actually one of the main take aways of this activity.
  • Page 2: Symmetric Cryptography.
    • Learning Goal:
      • Understand how Caesar and substitution ciphers work.
      • Understand the difference between a Caesar Cipher and a substitution cipher.
      • Gain insight into the advantages and weaknesses of these ciphers.
      • Anticipate the ways in which these ciphers can be broken.
      • Be able to explain why these ciphers are examples of symmetric cryptography.
    • Tips:
      • Some students may find the ciphers discussed here too abstract. Let them encode/decode a simple message using the example ciphers given to gain some hands-on experience.
      • Take some time to discuss the weaknesses of these ciphers and how they may be broken. Invite suggestions on how to improve on the security of such systems of cryptography.
  • Page 3: Caesar Cipher Project.
    • Learning Goal:
      • Gain hands-on experience with cryptography.
      • Implement a simple version of the Caesar Cipher.
      • Gain experience with the Unicode system of representing characters.
    • Tips:
      • Some students may have difficulty understanding the Unicode system. Encourage them to study the chart given which shows how each character have an associated Unicode number.
      • From the earlier page on symmetric cryptography, some students will be expecting that the Caesar cipher will wrap letters around the alphabet when shifting. Explain to them that the mathematics of emulating such wrapping is non-trivial and we will be implementing a simpler version of the Caesar cipher that does not involve any wrapping.
    • Page 4: Public Key Encryption.
      • Learning Goal: Understand the basics of public key encryption, secure HTTP, and certificate authorities.
    • Page 5: Cybersecurity.
      • Learning Goals:
        • Understand various types of cybercrime including distributed denial-of-service attacks, phishing, viruses, and antivirus software.
        • Understand the issues surrounding cybersecurity.
    • Page 6: Who Cares About Encryption?
      • Learning Goal: Understand the social implications of public key encryption, and be able to detail positive and negative effects.

Assignments:

  • Assessment Idea:
    • Describe a security attack and how cryptography helps protect against the attack.
