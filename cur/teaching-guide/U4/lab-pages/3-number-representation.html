Programming Lab 3: Number Representation

This lab is meant to address the requirements of AP CSP LO 2.1.2, "Explain how binary sequences are used to represent digital data." (But it does not cover hardware abstraction levels.)

The first page introduces binary representation as a place value system just like decimal representation, but with base 2 instead of 10.

The second page explains that a sequence of bits can represent things other than binary integers, and in particular explains floating point notation for real numbers.

The last page addresses hexadecimal (base 16), building on students' understanding of binary to support IPv6 addresses and RGB colors used in HTML/CSS.

Pacing:

The 3 lab pages could be split across 2 or 3 days (105-130 minutes). Expected times to complete follow:
  • 15-20 minutes (<1 class period) on Binary Representation
    • This lab page was originally included in Unit 1. If your students covered it there, you can skip it here.
  • 45-55 minutes (1 class period) on Binary Sequences
  • 45-55 minutes (1 class period) on Hexadecimal Representation

Prepare:

  • There's no such thing as a "binary number" or a "hexadecimal number." Numbers are the underlying idea of counts of things. If I hold 17 pencils in my hand and I then decide to think in hexadecimal and say I have 1116 pencils, that doesn't make some pencils magically disappear from my hand. I still have 17 of them. Instead, we speak of "binary numerals" or "binary representation." Many curriculum materials get this wrong. It's not just pedantry; students will really understand the conversions better if they don't have the misunderstanding that the number changes. The analogy with Roman numerals may be helpful; students are likely to understand and be able extend from the idea that MCMLXII and 1962 are the same number written in different representations.

Students will:

Looking Ahead
  • Students will create decimal-to-binary and binary-to-decimal converter blocks in Unit 6, Recursive Reporters.
  • Extend their understanding of different number bases to include binary and hexadecimal.
  • Develop a beginning understanding of floating point.
  • Explore hexadecimal representation and its use in RGB color and IP addresses.

As a Class:

  • Page 2: Binary Sequences.
    • Encourage students to take turns reading on this page.
  • Page 3: Hexadecimal Representation.
    • Don't try to teach students to do hexadecimal arithmetic. The goal is for students to become familiar with the notation of hex. Consider translating between representations for a few numbers together at the board.

Related Resources:

  • Students do not need to become proficient in conversion between representations, but some students may like to play one or more of the following number representation games. Offer them at your discretion, but watch that they do not become a distraction from the essential content of the unit.
    • Cisco Binary Game: a base 10 to base 2 board-clearing game. This game does have an element of time pressure and might take few minutes to figure out; the instructions are not perfect but helpful.
    • Bin-Hex Challenge: a base 2 to base 16 memory-style game. This game has no time pressure, so it might be the most accessible of the three. Also, the first ten matches (016 to 916) are easier because they are like converting between base 10 and base 2.
    • Flippy Bit And The Attack Of The Hexadecimals From Base 16: a base 2 to base 16 arcade-style shooter. This one is especially challenging and should only be offered to students with extra time or for play outside of class.

Tips:

  • Page 1: Binary Representation.
    • In addition to making the comparison between powers of two and powers of ten for the place values, this page pushes on the fact that there is a unique way to represent any positive integer as a sum of powers of two.
  • Page 2: Binary Sequences.
    • We explain that other data types, including lists, are represented as binary sequences, but we do not introduce the idea of pointers to memory addresses.
  • Page 3: Hexadecimal Representation.
    • Don't overemphasize number representations. This is a popular topic because it's easy, but it doesn't call for deep understanding; hex is just a notation that people find convenient in a few computing contexts. (Very few, actually; RGB and IPv6 pretty much exhaust them.)
    • Although base 16 is a base in its own right, it's important only as an abbreviation for the underlying binary. You don't necessarily have to say that to students, but don't allow a mystique to develop around hex.
    • Remind students that the purpose of computer science is to abstract away the details. That's why "orange" (which browsers also understand) is a much better representation for that color than "FF7F00."
