Programming Lab 1: Website Data

The lab begins with an introduction to the Internet that covers the difference between the World Wide Web and the Internet, URLs, the physical structure and history of Internet, network redundancy, and domain name hierarchy.

Later in Unit 4, students will work with the http block as they scrape and process data from web pages. The last two pages of this lab prepare students for these projects by introducing the basics of HTML, the concept of HTTP, and the Snap! http block. The goal is for students to gain very basic familiarity with HTML and web page scraping.

This lab also introduces a new lab page design element: "Do If There Is Time." This section is included to provide greater support for differentiation:

  • "For You to Do" problems should be completed by all students
  • "Do If There Is Time" should be attempted by most students
  • "Take It Further" is offered for those students who are moving most quickly.
The goal of this three-tiered approach is to help teachers keep all students moving through the lab pages at the same pace regardless of the pace of individual pairs.

Essential Knowledge:

  • EK 4.1.2E Some programming languages are designed for specific domains and are better for expressing algorithms in those domains.
  • EK 6.1.1A The Internet connects devices and networks all over the world.
  • EK 6.1.1G The domain name system (DNS) translates names to IP addresses.
  • EK 6.2.1A The Internet and the systems built on it are hierarchical and redundant.
  • EK 6.2.1B The domain name syntax is hierarchical.
  • EK 6.2.1D Routing on the Internet is fault tolerant and redundant.
  • EK 6.2.2A Hierarchy and redundancy help systems scale.
  • EK 6.2.2B The redundancy of routing (i.e., more than one way to route data) between two points on the Internet increases the reliability of the Internet and helps it scale to more devices and more people.
  • EK 6.2.2C Hierarchy in the DNS helps that system scale.
  • EK 6.2.2H Standards for sharing information and communicating between browsers and servers on the Web include HTTP and secure sockets layer/transport layer security (SSL/TLS).
