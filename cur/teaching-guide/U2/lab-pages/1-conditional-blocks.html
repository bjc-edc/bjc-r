Programming Lab 1: Conditional Blocks

Your ATM is programmed to let you choose whether to deposit or withdraw. The program does that by writing those choices somewhere on its screen and then testing to determine the location you touch or the button you press. The action the program takes or the choice it offers next is conditional; it depends on what input you've given it with your touch. Another part of the program may test the size of the withdrawal you type in, and indicate if that is too large, or not an amount it can give; or it may deliver that amount. Your smart phone's screen works a similar way. It knows only where you touch, but because it also knows what word or picture it put in that location, it can "know what you want"—if you touch here, do one thing, else do something else. Distinguishing conditions and basing action on the distinction is central to all programming.

In this lab, students focus attention on conditionals—especially if-else and if, but also repeat-until—to control the behavior of their programs based on whatever conditions the students test for, using predicates like < or =.

Small projects focus especially on using multiple if statements to distinguish multiple conditions. Students design a program that tests to see whether "red," "yellow," or "green" has been typed, and sets a traffic signal to that color; they design another program to distinguish various spellings of nouns and then decide, based on the condition they find, how to spell the plurals of those nouns. The first programs students write test to see whether the direction or y position of the sprite meet certain conditions, and base actions on the results.

This lab also begins to focus deep attention on abstraction through analyzing tasks to break them into subtasks and creating blocks that specialize in these subtasks, and on analyzing and debugging scripts. Analyzing an existing script is a particularly powerful way to learn to program similar behaviors. Debugging is an essential skill—especially as the programs one writes become more complex—but one that takes time to acquire and hone because there is no single formula or subskill for doing it well. In this lab, the debugging focus is on deliberately trying to "break" a program, searching for ways that a user's input (or some situation that arises on the stage) can cause a program to fail, and accounting for those ways by testing for them and responding appropriately.

Pacing:

  • This week contains two labs. Though they differ in page length, each will probably take roughly half the time you have available.
  • The goal is for students to learn and succeed, not rush, and to have chances to try some of the Take It Further problems and to be creative on their own. Plan accordingly, but...
  • ...if you notice that the first page of Lab 1 is taking longer than you had anticipated, consider doing only problems 1-3 on page 2 (Using Multiple If statements) and skipping the rest of that page (problems 4 through 7). There will be other opportunities to work with multiple ifs.

Prepare:

  • Review all lab activities beforehand.
  • Prepare a Computing in the News item.
  • In teaching the first day: Make sure that students don't skip over the "figure out how it works" part of problem 1 on the "Getting Started" page. Figuring out how sample code works, not just seeing what it does, is a powerful way to learn to code. In this case, the figuring out helps students do problems 2 and 3 on that page. It's probably not necessary to do anything formal with that "figure out" part—talking with the pair programmer is likely to be enough—but if you feel your students need more structure, you might prepare a paper copy of the scripts for students to annotate, or some other way to make the results of problem 1 more tangible than discussion.

Students will:

Looking Ahead

Take-It-Further problems 5 and 6 on the last page of this lab—Plurals, continued—foreshadow recursion, an extremely powerful idea that students will study formally later. Students develop a block plural to spell the plural of an English noun, creating separate blocks for various special cases (nouns ending with s, x, h, y, and so on) and otherwise just appending s. Then they fix plural to handle a "word ending with a space" and use plural, itself, as the specialist to handle the word after that space has been removed. It may surprise them to see that this solution works even with "words" that end with more than one space.

  • Analyze scripts to understand how they work and to learn to use those methods for their own scripts.
  • Develop predicates to test for various conditions and use conditional blocks to control programs based on those conditions.
  • Analyze tasks to find subtasks that can be handled independently, develop blocks that specialize in each of the subtasks, and use conditionals to determine which of these specialist blocks to use.
  • Use abstraction to simplify and clarify code.
  • Debug code by deliberately trying to find ways to "break" it.

As a Class:

  • Except where you may choose to have a "Computing in the News" discussion or have students share techniques they used or particularly interesting results they got, this lab can productively be all pair-programming work.

Look For:

  • Always look for pairs in which students are not getting the help that they need from their partner.
  • Look for students who elaborate the tasks, or creatively produce new tasks or have other ideas worth sharing with the class.
