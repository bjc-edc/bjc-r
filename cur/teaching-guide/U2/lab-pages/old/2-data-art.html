Lab 2: Making Art by Using Data Structures

Students have already encountered variables in two contexts: they have created blocks that have input variables, and have used those within the block's script; and they have used the for block, which provides a numeric variable for students to use in their scripts.

This lab introduces the block, which lets programmers create their own temporary variables for scripts, variables in which to store, use, or modify information that the script will need while it is running. Script variables exist only while the script is running and only inside that script. Blocks used inside a script can set, use, or change the value of that script's variables; blocks outside of that script cannot.

Students get a choice of small projects in art or language or mathematics and get plenty of practice building blocks of various types including predicates.

Prepare:

  • Getting, renaming, setting, and using a script variable are all new. Some students will need help just building the script shown in problem 1 on the first page of this lab. An animation shows most of what they will need to do, but make sure you are comfortable enough, yourself, to help if that is needed.
  • With other problems, it can actually be useful for you not to look fully fluent, even if you know exactly what to do. Showing how you puzzle out the problem can be an excellent way of modeling the thinking for your students. You don't have to "fake" not knowing. Your genuine curiosity about what the student has already done is likely to be enough. Ask what various bits of the program do, or what they're supposed to do, or how the student figured out what to put in or where to put it. You experiment with the student's program, pausing for a moment to think about what it did. Or suggest trying various inputs to see how the program responds. These can be enormously helpful to the student because they illustrate a good analysis/debugging technique. Often enough, students then find the solutions themselves.

    • Mathematics Note: Factorial. Problem 5 hints at an algorithm inspired by "5 factorial is really just 5 times 4 factorial." A Snap! model is:

      The factorial function has many uses in mathematics, most of them involving counting problems. For example, suppose you have a list of four letters: a, b, c, and d. You may have noticed that the order of the elements in a list matters:

      How many different lists, then, can you make out of the four letters, using each only once? Well, there are four choices for the first element of the list. Once you have picked one, there are 3 choices for the second element. So there are are 4 × 3 ways to arrange the first two elements. For each of these 12 arrangements, there are two letters left for the third element of your list and only one letter left to go in the last slot. Hence, there are 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 or 4! possible lists from the four letters. (You might ask your students to generate them all with a Snap! block.) And there are 5! lists that can be made from 5 distinct letters. This is typical of how factorial is used—counting the number of lists that can be made from a collection of distinct elements.

  • Mathematics Note: Counting Divisors. The function that counts the number of divisors, number-of-divisors, is part of a family of functions that share some wonderful properties. One such property: if two integers m and n have no common factor other than 1, then the number of divisors of mn is the product of the number of divisors of n and the number of divisors of m. For example, the number of divisors of 6 is 4 (its divisors are 1, 2, 3, and 6), and the number of divisors if 5 is 2 (1 and 5). The number of divisors of 30 (6 × 5) is 8 (4 × 2). Once again, it’s a counting issue: each divisor of 30 comes from a divisor of 6 multiplied by a divisor of 5.
  • Mathematics Note: Divisibility by 3 or 9. These divisibility tests (in the "Take It Further" section) can be explained by looking at the way numbers are made up in base 10. For example: 372 = 3 \times 100 + 7 \times 10 + 2 Take sum of the digits (3 + 7 + 2) and subtract it from 372, but do it in a way that "delays the evaluation" so that you can see the structure:

    This will work for any integer (try it). So, any integer minus the sum of its digits is divisible by 9. This means that an integer is divisible by 9 if and only if the sum of its digits is divisible by 9.

    And the reasoning says something even stronger than this: not only is a number divisible by 9 if and only if the sum of its digits is, these two numbers are the same mod 9. That is, an integer and the sum of its digits differ by a multiple of 9.
  • Mathematics Note: Divisibility by 7. The divisibility test for 7 works differently. In the chain 50197 \rightarrow 50113 \rightarrow 5005 \rightarrow 490 \rightarrow 44 \rightarrow -7 each number number is divisible by 7. But if you apply the test to a number that’s not divisible by 7, say 2012, you get a chain: 2012 \rightarrow 197 \rightarrow 5005 \rightarrow 5 and each number does not leave the same remainder when divided by 7. The result (5) shows that none of them are divisible by 7, and that’s all it shows. The reason this works is a little complicated, but there have been two papers in the Mathematics Teacher that delve into the reasons (one by a BJC author):
    • "How does one know if a number is divisible by 17?" by Paul Goldenberg (Mathematics Teacher, March 2006)
    • "Divisibility Tests" by Allen Olsen (Mathematics Teacher, August 2006)
