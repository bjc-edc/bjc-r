Programming Lab 3: Developing More Complex Programs

Throughout Lab 3, students focus almost exclusively on ideas of abstraction to serve the goals writing clear, debuggable, improvable code in order to develop more complex programs.

There is little new snap code in Lab 3. Nor is the idea of abstraction—analyzing a task to think about what its chunks or component parts or special cases might be and then building blocks that specialize in each of them—really new. Students did that when they built plural. But learning to think that way takes time and experience. In this lab, students work on three projects. Both the game board project and the number-guessing-game project are very extensible and modifiable and may lead students to their own ideas.

The lab ends with two shorter activities—essentially how-to's—showing techniques that can be useful in more complex programming tasks.

Prepare:

  • Search for some Computing in the News topics, or ask your students to look for them in advance.

Students will:

Looking Ahead
  • The tools developed in this unit anticipate a variety of games that students will be able to develop: tic-tac-toe, magic-square games, and others. More powerful programming techniques—particularly lists which are the major new programming idea in Unit 3—are useful in completing these games.
  • Build some tools for board games: a block to draw square arrays of various sizes for the game board, and a block that determines which cell within that array where the sprite is in.
  • Build a number guessing game in which the user tries to guess the computer's secret number, and the computer tries to respond with as human-like dialogue as the programmer can build.
  • Learn how to specify the type of input that a block is expecting to receive.
  • Get a bit of practice composing functions.

As a Class:

  • Except for a Computing in the News, and the usual option of having students share particularly interesting problems or results, treat this lab as largely work-with-your-partner time.

Look For:

  • As always, look for students who have interesting result to share.
  • As always, look for students who seem stuck and are not getting the support that they need from their partner .
