GPS Data

Big Burgers is coming to New York City, and asked people to share their location to decide where to put their new restaurants.

Hundreds of people have shared their locations through GPS coordinates.

Locations in this format are called Decimal Degrees:
DDD.DDDD° (latitude, north or south of the Equator), DDD.DDDD° (longitude, east or west of the Prime Meridian through London).
If you search for "40.748817, -73.985428" in Google Maps, you can see the corresponding location, near the Empire State Building.

It's not necessary, but if you are interested, read the following quick introduction on how GPS coordinates work: E-How Tutorial on GPS coordinates.

The data given to you are 10 GPS coordinates, which can be used to determine a location on the Earth. Each row is a separate coordinate, consisting of two numbers, a latitude followed by a longitude.

Most computer based mapping systems display this format for GPS coordinates. Positive values of latitude are north of the equator, negative values to the south. Most programs (not all) use negative values for west longitude.

Transform the data

Transform these GPS coordinates into coordinates that will display nicely on this x-y grid. These problems will go through one way to do this (there's more than one way).
    Yes, subtracting -122.27 is the same as adding 122.27.
  1. Every x-coordinate starts with "37.86" and every y-coordinate begins with "-122.27". This information is not helping us tell the points apart. Subtract 37.86 from every x-coordinate and subtract -122.27 from every y-coordinate, using a higher-order function.
  2. Now the first x-coordinate is 0.009050. There are many choices we could make to turn this into a "nicer" number. One choice is to multiply every x- and y-coordinate by 1000000 (that's one million). Adjust your higher-order function to do this calculation as well.
    3. Making Snap! do the work is better, because then the program can be used on any set of GPS points in this area, and not just these. That's abstraction.
    Recall that you made a block called average in Lab 2 ("Combine with").
  3. Now the first x-coordinate is 9050, which we still can't see on the grid. Let's center the coordinates around the average x- and y-coordinate. Subtract the average of all the x values from each of the x-coordinates, and subtract the average y values from each of the y-coordinates. You can find the average on a calculator, or even better, make Snap! do the work.
  4. Once you have these transformed coordinates, use your script from problem 1 to graph the points.
