Abstraction Inside the Computer

In Unit 4, you saw that the Internet software in a computer is arranged in several levels of abstraction, with application programs (like your email program) at the highest level and transport protocols such as WiFi and Ethernet at the lowest level. Recall that higher levels of abstraction are closer to what users want to be thinking about; lower levels are closer to the way machines work.

The way a computer works on the inside also has levels of abstraction. In this lab, you will learn about these levels.

Software domain. At the highest level are programs: some people write them, some people just use them. For the most part, neither the programmer nor the user thinks at all about how the wires, glass, and plastic—the hardware—inside a smartphone or computer actually fit together or work. This is an abstraction—a way of thinking that takes the computer itself for granted without thinking about how it works.

Similarly, a driver is thinking at a high level of abstraction when using the brake pedal. As long as the engineers and mechanics designed and built the car right, drivers don't need to know how the brake system works.

Digital domain. Below the programming level is hardware (the guts of the computer) and its architecture (the design that specifies how those basic hardware components fit together to do their job). The hardware's job is to execute whatever programs it gets. For hardware engineers, the high level of abstraction focuses on how to make best use of components (memory, chips, logic gates, etc.) that already have some useful built in behaviors. And they, too, often assemble these into slightly fancier components that they will need. These designers don't (mostly) have to think about how these digital components work inside. At this level, designers think of circuits being either on or off, 1 or 0, with no in-between values.

Analog domain. We call digital an abstraction because at the lowest level, "noise" in the electronic circuits means that there are in-between values. Electrical engineers must think about the laws of electronics (physics) to design circuits so that these in-between values stay close enough to 0 or 1 to be treated as if they really are 0 or 1.
