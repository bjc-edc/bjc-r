Abstraction Inside the Computer

In Unit 4, you saw that the Internet software in a computer is arranged in several levels of abstraction, with application programs (like your email program) at the highest level and transport protocols such as WiFi and Ethernet at the lowest level. Recall that higher levels of abstraction are closer to what users want to be thinking about; lower levels are closer to the way machines work.

What's going on inside a computer also has levels of abstraction.

At the highest level are programs: some people write them, some people just use them. For the most part, neither the programmer nor the user thinks at all about how the wires, glass, and plastic—the hardware—inside a smartphone or computer actually fit together or work. Programming and programs are an "abstraction"—a way of thinking that ignores details that are, for current purposes, irrelevant and distracting.

Similarly, in a car, knowing that the brake pedal slows the car down is a high level of abstraction; as long as the engineers and mechanics designed and built the car right, you don't need to know how the brake pedal causes wheels to slow down.

Programs start out as ideas in your head; a programming language lets you express those ideas in a form that can be carried out by a computer. The programming language—any programming language—provides basic parts with built-in behaviors that you, the programmer, just assemble in order to accomplish your purpose. Often, you assemble those very basic parts into bigger parts that your program will need, like , and then you think in terms of these even higher levels of abstraction.

Below the programming level is hardware, the guts of the computer and its "architecture," the way it is designed. The hardware's job is to execute whatever programs it gets. But even computer architects are (mostly) thinking just about how to make best use of parts (memory, chips, logic gates, etc.) that already have some useful built in behaviors. And they, too, often assemble these into slightly fancier components that they will need. These designers don't (mostly) have to think about the physics of electrons or photons.

At the lowest level, computer scientists, mathematicians, and physicists who are inventing completely new ways to design faster, smaller computers do need to pay attention to the basic physics that determines how the hardware components work.

Roughly, the abstraction levels might look like this:

    application program        software
    operating system        software
------------------    PROGRAM ABSTRACTION    
    stored-program architecture        architecture
    component level: processor, memory, input/output        hardware/architecture
    integrated circuits (chips)        hardware/architecture
    logic gates        hardware
    DIGITAL ABSTRACTION    
    transistors        a most basic component
    quantum physics        mathematics

Different people might draw this diagram slightly differently, so don't try to memorize all the levels. The key abstraction levels to remember are programming, computer hardware, and underlying physics.
