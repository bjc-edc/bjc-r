The First Computer

When was the first computer built?

This is actually a trick question, because the answer depends on where you draw the line between computers and non-computers.

20,000 years ago people cut patterns of notches into animal bones. Some experts believe that these tally sticks were used to perform arithmetic computations.

An abacus.
By HB - Own work, Public Domain, from Wikimedia.

The abacus was first invented about 4,000 years ago. It is a computing device that can be used to do arithmetic, even including square roots and cube roots, on multi-digit numbers. In some countries, abaci are still widely used today. People who are well-trained to use the abacus can perform calculations remarkably quickly, given that human fingers have to move the stones that represent each digit. But you may not want to call this a "computer," since it's still a human brain directing the operation of the machine.

An orrery

There have been many inventions, over the course of history, of special-purpose computing devices. A typical example is the orrery, a device made to display the positions of the planets in the Solar System. The first known device of this kind was the Antikythera mechanism from about 2100 years ago. It was discovered in 1900, in fragments, in an ancient shipwreck in Greece. At the time it was made, it was generally believed that the sun and the other planets all revolved around the Earth. This made the positions very complicated to work out, so the mechanism was much harder to design and build than it would be today. (The sun-centered understanding of the Solar System was introduced to European science by Nicolaus Copernicus in 1543. The first sun-centered orrery was made in about 1704.)

These mechanisms were a kind of computer, but they weren't programmable. They could only do one particular computation that was built into their mechanism.
