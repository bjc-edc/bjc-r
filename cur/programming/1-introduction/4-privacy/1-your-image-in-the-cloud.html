In this lab, you will learn about your digital privacy.

On this page, you will consider what information is available online about you and reflect on your online data sharing habits.

IOC-1.F.11 bullet 3, IOC-2.A.5
IOC-2.A.1

Personally identifiable information (PII) is information that might make it possible for someone to figure out who you are, when you're trying not to allow that, such as your Social Security number, age, race, phone number(s), medical information, financial information, or biometric data (such as your thumbprint or face scan).

There are many ways that personally identifiable information about you can fall into the hands of someone you didn't intend to have it. If you post a picture of you and your friends to a website, you are making information public about where you were and what you were doing. This may violate both your own privacy and that of your friends. Also, if your phone is lost or stolen, someone else might get access to pictures or messages you thought were private. That's why data on cell phones is normally encrypted so that it can't be recovered without your password. Having to type your password is a trade-off between convenience and security. But some recent research has shown that cell phone apps are often written to collect information about you and your contacts and location without your knowledge, mostly to sell targeted advertising.

IOC-2.A.8, IOC-2.A.9

There are some benefits to PII being shared online. For example, it can be used to enhance your online experiences (such as to show you personalized movie recommendations), and it can also simplify online purchasing (such as by suggesting things you are likely to buy based on previous purchases). However, there are also reasons not to share PII online.

What laws exist to protect PII?

In the United States, the law says that web sites must have a privacy policy that spells out what the site will do with the information you give them. This is a pretty weak protection; the privacy policies are typically really long, and written by lawyers, so it's hard to find out what information they collect and what they do with it. In Europe, there is now a General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) that spells out what counts as PII, and says that web sites can't do certain things, regardless of what they put in their privacy policies, without your affirmative permission (you have to say yes, not merely not say no), and that they can't deny otherwise free services to you because of your refusal to permit the use they want you to agree to.

The GDPR is new enough that its implications are still being worked out. For example, to get a Snap! account, you have to provide an email address, your own or your parent's depending on your age. That's the only thing Snap! knows about you: not your name, not your home address, just your email address. Is that PII? Some lawyers think it is. Other lawyers think that your email alone isn't PII, but it is when combined with certain other information. If it's PII, then Snap! has to ask if you're in Europe; if so, it has to collect more information about you, such as your name, age, and address, to be able to satisfy some of the GDPR requirements. Your age matters, clearly, because the rules are different for children and adults. (In Europe, the cutoff age is 16, whereas in the US, we have to ask for your parent's email if you're below 13.) The rest of the PII we have to know is to satisfy the requirement that if you ask to have your account deleted, we must do so at once. So we have to be able to make sure that it's really you, or your parent, asking, not some friend wanting to delete your account as a prank.

Which is safer: browsers or phones?

If you're like most people, when you are on a computer you use a web browser (such as Chrome, Safari, Edge) to connect to all websites you visit. But on your phone, you may have a separate app installed for some sites (such as Instagram or Facebook). From a privacy point of view, using a browser is much better.

  • In a browser, you can install privacy extensions (such as AdBlockPlus, Ghostery, or NoScript) that restrict the ability of web sites to gather your information. With a site-specific app, there's no mechanism to let you change how it works.
  • On a computer, when you exit from a browser, it stops running. (This doesn't have to be true, but when a browser company tries to get around that rule, someone catches them at it, and people stop using that browser for a while.) But almost all phone apps keep running even when you're not using them, and many of them are constantly accessing your contacts, messages, and other data and sending that information over the Internet to the company that made the app. That company may even be in a country with very weak privacy protection.
  • On a computer, the standard thing is to store your information on the computer's own hard drive. On a phone, the standard thing is to store your information in "the cloud," i.e., on computers belonging to your cell phone carrier (Verizon, ATT, etc.) or the phone manufacturer (Apple, Samsung, Google, etc.).

IOC-2.A.7
There are even companies whose sole business is to collect information about you from other companies, large or small, that you use online. But the biggest data gatherers are Google, cell phone carriers, Facebook, and the US government.

"What does organization X know about you?" is kind of a trick question because many these companies and institutions share the information they collect about you. The sharing of data among different companies is important—and problematic—because different kinds of information, while each unimportant separately, can turn into a serious privacy problem when combined. For example, your cell phone carrier knows everyplace you go while Google knows what people or businesses live at every address; in combination, these two kinds of information can reveal whether you've visiting a divorce lawyer, an AIDS doctor, or other possibilities that could be embarrassing (or worse) if revealed.
