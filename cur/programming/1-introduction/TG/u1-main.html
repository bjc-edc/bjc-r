Purpose

AP CS Principles Learning Objectives are organized into seven "Big Ideas":
1 Creativity
2 Abstraction
3 Data and Information
4 Algorithms
5 Programming
6 The Internet
7 Global Impact
Students dive right into using snap by exploring its features and creating visual projects using important programming ideas such as loops, randomness, and building blocks. As much as possible, students should be encouraged to experiment with snap, playing with input parameters, using blocks before they have been formally introduced, and modifying and extending the assigned tasks. Creativity is not only a Big Idea of AP CS Principles, but also essential to students' experience of the course. In Unit 1, students will also discuss social implications of computing, (NEED TO EDIT) including issues such as cyberbullying, the prevalence of social media, and the ethics of illegally downloading music. Students are likely to be familiar with these issues, and grappling with them at the beginning of the course helps students understand the relevance and necessity of understanding the world of computing.

Highlighting Computational Thinking

AP CS Principles identifies six Computational Thinking Practices:
P1 Connecting Computing
P2 Creating Computational Artifacts
P3 Abstracting
P4 Analyzing Problems and Artifacts
P5 Communicating
P6 Collaborating
One way students develop their computational thinking skills is through direct experience with snap. For example, students are frequently asked to predict what they think will result from a given snap script. Making such predictions requires students to make sense of the sequential nature of the code. Students are introduced to the idea of abstraction, and will experience this way of thinking as they learn what details they can manipulate or safely overlook in a script as they explore it, and as they are introduced to functions as a way to generalize processes. The emphasis on Pair Programming in Unit 1 is meant to help students communicate and collaborate in problem solving. Finally, students engage with the broader world of computing in exploring topics related to its impacts. As students write about and debate important social issues, they develop the thinking skills required to analyze complex issues.


Week 1 (Programming Focus)

In preparation for Week 1:
  • Make your own snap account using your classroom's technology
  • Think about and set up other technology (Google classroom, Piazza, etc.) you'd like students to use
  • Read this article about pair programming.
  • Check out these tips from csteachingtips.org:
    • Have students sign a "Computer Use Agreement" before giving them access to machines to hold them accountable for using computers responsibly.
    • Encourage students to talk to each other to decrease the burden on the teacher and to emulate industry work environments.
  • Know how to save, submit, and share projects in snap
  • During class, take note of interesting approaches or creative ideas that would be interesting to share. fConsider also asking students to submit yet-incomplete kaleidoscopes (give them some way of indicating this in the file name) if you see an interesting intermediate attempt that would spark discussion.

Week 3 (Social Implications Focus)

In preparation for Week 3:
  • There are many options for students to access Blown to Bits. They can read it as an online PDF or you may choose to purchase hard copies of the book. You may also photocopy the book for students so that they can make annotations as they read.
  • Other suggestions
Students discuss and write about issues of Community and Online Interactions and Privacy.
  • Social Implications Lab 7: Privacy (student pages) (teacher support) ~ 120 minutes
    • Discuss: Why do we need privacy? What is the case against privacy?
    • Discuss: What are the different ways our privacy is affected by computers?
    • Read: Blown to Bits: "Footprints and Fingerprints"
    • Discuss: What are some innovations (good and bad) that we give information to?
    • Read: "Addicted to Apps"
  • Social Implications Lab 8: Community and Online Interactions (student pages) (teacher support) ~ 120 minutes
    • Discuss: What do you know about how Twitter works? What questions do you have about how it works?
    • Discuss: What are some different ways we communicate?
    • Write: Choose a good innovation and say why ok to give up privacy for it. And choose an innovation and say why we should be cautious of how much privacy we give up for it.
    • Respond: Read each others' writing and write a response.
