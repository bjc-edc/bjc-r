Purpose

Students dive right into using snap by exploring its features and creating visual projects using important programming ideas such as loops, randomness, and building blocks. As much as possible, students should be encouraged to experiment with snap, playing with input parameters, using blocks before they have been formally introduced, and modifying and extending the assigned tasks. Creativity is not only a Big Idea of AP CS Principles, but also essential to students' experience of the course. In Unit 1, students will also discuss social implications of computing, including issues such as cyberbullying, the prevalence of social media, and the ethics of downloading illegal music. Students are likely to be familiar with these issues, and grappling with them at the beginning of the course helps students understand the relevance and necessity of understanding the world of computing.

Highlighting Computational Thinking

AP CS Principles identifies six Computational Thinking Practices:
  1. P1: Connecting Computing
  2. P2: Creating Computational Artifacts
  3. P3: Abstracting
  4. P4: Analyzing Problems and Artifacts
  5. P5: Communicating
  6. P6: Collaborating
One way students develop their computational thinking skills is through direct experience with snap. For example, students are frequently asked to predict what they think will result from a given snap script. Making such predictions requires students to make sense of the sequential nature of the code. Students are introduced to the idea of abstraction, and will experience this way of thinking as they learn what details they can manipulate or safely overlook in a script as they explore it, and as they are introduced to functions as a way to generalize processes. The emphasis on Pair Programming in Unit 1 is meant to help students communicate and collaborate in problem solving. Finally, students engage with the broader world of computing in exploring topics related to its impacts.
