Ways to Think About It

Suppose you call . Ther reporter has to insert six numbers (11, 12, 13, 14, 15, and 16) into the empty list. Now increase the upper limit to 32. The reporter now needs to insert the integers from 11 to 32 into the empty list, and there are 12 of them (count them if you are skeptical).

Well, the data-plot approximates a line. Why do you think it's approximate?

And if you call list between with inputs 11 and 64, how many numbers does it need to insert? If you scale the upper limit by 2, the average time for the output gets scaled by 2. And, in general, if you scale the upper limit by some number, the average time for the output gets scaled by the same number. By Morgan's last comment, the graph is line.

Computer scientists (cleverly) call these algorithms linear-time algorithms. Linear-time algorithms are also much sought-after in computer science, and for many problems, they are the fastest algorithms you can find.
