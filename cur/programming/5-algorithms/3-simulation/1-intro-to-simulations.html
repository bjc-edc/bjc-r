2.3 Models and simulations use abstraction to generate new understanding and knowledge.
2.3.1 Use models and simulations to represent phenomena. [P3]
2.3.1A Models and simulations are simplified representations of more complex objects or phenomena.
2.3.1B Models may use different abstractions or levels of abstraction depending on the objects or phenomena being posed.
2.3.1C Models often omit unnecessary features of the objects or phenomena that are being modeled.
2.3.1D Simulations mimic real world events without the cost or danger of building and testing the phenomena in the real world.
2.3.2 Use models and simulations to formulate, refine, and test hypotheses. [P3]
2.3.2A Models and simulations facilitate the formulation and refinement of hypotheses related to the objects or phenomena under consideration.
2.3.2B Hypotheses are formulated to explain the objects or phenomena being modeled.
2.3.2C Hypotheses are refined by examining the insights that models and simulations provide into the objects or phenomena.
2.3.2D The results of simulations may generate new knowledge and new hypotheses related to the phenomena being modeled.
2.3.2E Simulations allow hypotheses to be tested without the constraints of the real world.
2.3.2F Simulations can facilitate extensive and rapid testing of models.
2.3.2G The time required for simulations is impacted by the level of detail and quality of the models, and the software and hardware used for the simulation.
2.3.2H Rapid and extensive testing allows models to be changed to accurately reflect the objects or phenomena being modeled.

Models and simulations are computer representations of complex things in the real world. They are used to explain or predict the actual behaviors of objects in the real world. Why use simulations, instead of doing experiments? There are many reasons; here are a few:

  • Doing the experiment in the real world may be very expensive, requiring the construction of, say, an airplane that might fall apart in strong winds. Modeling the airplane shape and simulating the wind behavior can eliminate some bad designs until reaching a level of confidence that justifies finally building the real airplane.
  • Doing the experiment may be very dangerous, such as testing whether a nuclear reactor will survive an earthquake.
  • Doing the experiment may be unethical, such as giving people a disease to test whether a new drug will cure them.

Computer models rarely capture the full complexity of real situations. Think about the models that scientists use to predict the results of global climate change in 10 years, or in 100 years. So many factors affect the weather! Models have to account for geological fault lines that lead to earthquakes, the course of rivers, wind patterns, which plants and animals live in what regions, and much more. So model-builders make simplifying assumptions, based on their experience of which things were most important in past predictions.

Researchers may use an iterative design process, starting with a very simple model, and examining the results of simulations using that model to decide what aspects of the real situation have to be modeled more precisely for the next simulation. Really detailed models may push the limits of current computer speeds. That's another reason researchers may have to limit the complexity of the model. Complex models and simulations generally depend heavily on the idea of levels of abstraction. World climate is a good example of something that could never be simulated if programmers had to think about the lowest-level details all the time.

Explain how modeling and simulations may help with our understanding of these phenomena. What are the pros and cons of such an approach?
