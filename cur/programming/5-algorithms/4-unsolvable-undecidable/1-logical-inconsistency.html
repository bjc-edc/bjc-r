Logical Inconsistency

  • Logic puzzles
    Puzzles like these are famous among mathematicians and computer scientists. In fact, there's a whole branch of mathematics devoted to the logic that is behind puzzles like these.
  1. Imagine an island somewhere, with two large families. One family (unlike us "normals") can tell only the truth, even when they'd rather lie. The Truthteller family can't ever make false statements, even by mistake. The False-Teller family is just as reliable, but in the opposite way: they can't make true statements. Ever.

    You are visiting the island and meet two of its people, Adam and Eve.

    Adam says "Eve and I are from the same family."

    Can you say for sure what family Eve is from?

    Can you say for sure what family Adam is from?

  2. You meet someone Derek and ask him if he's from the Truthteller family. What does he answer? What if you ask Derek if he's from the False-Teller family?
Betsy: The statement I'm making right now is false.
Gamal: (in a bored tone) Whatever you say, Betsy.
Gamal thinks a moment.
Gamal: Wait! What??!!!!
Alphie: I can't believe the two of you!
Gamal: Hey, Betsy, that's cool!
Alphie sighs and walks off to lunch.
  1. Betsy claims her statement is False? What do you think? Make sure you can explain your idea clearly.
Some statements are inconsistent, self-contradictory, and can be neither True nor False. Some, like Adam's statement, cannot be determined with the information we have so far, but could be either True or False.
  1. What questions can you ask to determine, for sure, whether a person is a Truthteller or a False-Teller? (Talking with others, figure out at least four questions that will work reliably.)
  2. You've developed a "test." If Adam were a Truthteller, how would he answer your questions? Check to make sure that if he were a False-Teller, he'd answer differently.
Problem 5 asked you to devise a "test" to determine whether the person you've met is a Truthteller or a False-Teller. Some statements, as you will see in part 2 of this lab, could be True or False, but we can prove that no test could be devised to determine the Truth or Falsity of the statement.
  1. On that island of Truthtellers and False-Tellers, you meet Max and Min. Max says "She and I are both liars!" Which kind of statement is this? Is it self-contradictory? Is it indeterminate (it could be either, but there's no way to tell)? Or is it definitely resolvable?
