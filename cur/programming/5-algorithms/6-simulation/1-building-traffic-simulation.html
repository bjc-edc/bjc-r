Building a Traffic Simulation

Have you ever been on a highway going really slowly in heavy traffic and all of a sudden the traffic begins to speed up for no apparent reason at all? Nobody got off, you didn't pass an accident, nothing changed except that now the cars are going faster. In this lab, you will develop a model of what might affect traffic speed, and build a simulation in Snap! to explore that model.
To keep the model from being too complicated to program, we will make several simplifying assumptions.
  • There is a single lane of traffic and cars cannot pass each other.
  • The highway is straight and there are no stoplights, speed-change signs, or obstacles.
  • Drivers change speed to fit the traffic.
  • Drivers make sure that they don't crash into a car ahead of them, slowing gradually unless it is urgent to slow down more rapidly.
  • Drivers speed up whenever they can, up to the speed limit.
  • All cars have the same ability to speed up and slow down, equally good brakes, equally good engines, and equally good drivers.
  • Since the stage is not as wide as Arizona, and since we don't want to create 100,000 cars (sprites), we will recycle cars as they leave the stage, starting the same car at the beginning of the stage.
  • We can decide how many cars to put on the road.
  1. Analyze the state of one vehicle. What variables will it need? All cars will have the same rate of deceleration and acceleration, but that should be a variable so that we can experiment with it. Each car will have its own speed. Each car is following one particular other car. What, if any, other variables must each car keep track of?
  2. Analyze the behavior of one vehicle: If a driver sees a car in front of it, the driver slows to the speed of that car. Otherwise, the driver speeds up. Distance to the car in front should probably matter.
  3. Design an algorithm for the behavior: Exactly how will the car's speed change depending on the distance to the car in front of it?
  4. Program your algorithm. Give one sprite the ability to detect its distance to the (not-yet-existent) sprite ahead of it, and the script that uses that to control its forward motion.
  5. Clone your sprite to create enough cars for your simulation.
  6. Initialize: Set your cars out in some starting position, and give each car whatever other information it needs to start off.
  7. Test and refine your model: Do the cars keep moving? Do they avoid crashes as they should? Does traffic flow smoothly? Do you see changes in speed? Adjust your model, if necessary, so that it works.
