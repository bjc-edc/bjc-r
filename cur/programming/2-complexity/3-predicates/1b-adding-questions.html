The block reports the remainder when the first input is divided by the second. For example, reports 2 because when 17 is divided by 5, the remainder is 2. When one number divides another evenly, the remainder is 0. So, reports 0.

You may see these four arithmetic operators: 
+
, 
-
, 
*
, 
/
(plus, minus, times, divide) as well as 
MOD
. Arithmetic operators are part of most programming languages.
Although in Snap! you determine the order of operations by how you nest blocks inside each other (such as or ), in a text language when you write an expression such as 
3 * 5 + 4
, the conventional order of operations applies.
Different programming languages have different ways of handling negative inputs to the mod function.
The expression would be written as 
17 MOD 5
. If you see an expression like 
a MOD b
, you can assume that a \ge 0 and b > 0 (that is, that both inputs are positive, and that b can't be 0 because you can't divide by 0).
