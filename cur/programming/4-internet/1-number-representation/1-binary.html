Binary Representation

2.1.1D Number bases, including binary, decimal, and hexadecimal, are used to represent and investigate digital data.
2.1.1E At one of the lowest levels of abstraction, digital data is represented in binary (base 2) using only combinations of the digits zero and one.

While people typically work with numbers using the base 10 numeral system, other systems are relevant in computer science, including binary (base 2) and hexadecimal (base 16). Computers manage data packed as sequences of bits, binary data that are all zeros or ones. People are most familiar with base 10, so we write software that allows people to use base 10 to communicate with the computer.

In base 10, there are ten digits (0-9), each place is worth ten times the place to its right.

In binary, base 2, there are only two digits (0 and 1), each place is worth two times the place to its right.

The subscript 2 on 11012 means the 1101 is written in base 2. Since numbers are normally written in base 10, a subscript 10 is usually only used when needed for clarity.

In base 10 notation, each place value represents a power of ten: the units place (100 = 1), the tens place (101 = 10), the hundreds place (102 = 100), the thousands place (103 = 1000), etc. So, for example:

9827   =   9 × 103  +  8 × 102  +  2 × 101  +  7 × 100

The subscript 2 in 100102 means the 10010 is in binary. The subscript 10 in 1810 means the 18 is in decimal.

Base 2 uses the same idea but with powers of two instead of powers of ten. Binary place values represent: the units place (20 = 1), the twos place (21 = 2), the fours place (22 = 4), the eights place (23 = 8), sixteens place (24 = 16), etc. So, for example:

100102   =   1 × 24  +  0 × 23  +  0 × 22  +  1 × 21  +  0 × 20   =   16  +  2   =   1810

Here's an algorithm you can follow to find the binary representation of any base 10 integer:
You'll learn about how other real numbers are stored soon).
  1. First, find the largest power of two that fits inside the number.
  2. Then, subtract that power of 2 from the number, keep the new number, and record a 1 in the place for that power of 2.
  3. Then, determine if the next smallest power of 2 fits inside the new number, and:
    • If it does, subtract that power of 2 from the number, keep the new number, and record a 1 in the place for that power of 2.
    • If it doesn't, keep the same number, and record a 0 for that power of 2.
    Repeat this whole step for with next smallest power of 2 until you have a bit (1 or 0) for all the remaining places down to and including the ones place (by which point you should have nothing left of the original number).
    You could create a program based on an algorithm like this.

The string of ones and zeros you have recorded is the binary representation of your original number.
