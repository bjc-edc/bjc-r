Binary Representation

2.1.1D Number bases, including binary, decimal, and hexadecimal, are used to represent and investigate digital data.
2.1.1E At one of the lowest levels of abstraction, digital data is represented in binary (base 2) using only combinations of the digits zero and one.

While people typically work with numbers using the familiar base 10 (decimal) numeral system, other systems are relevant in computer science, including binary (base 2) and hexadecimal (base 16). Although computers interface with people using decimal numerals, computers actually manage data (numbers, text, images, etc.) in binary sequences of ones and zeros.

In base 10, there are ten digits (0-9), each place is worth ten times the place to its right.

In binary, base 2, there are only two digits (0 and 1), each place is worth two times the place to its right.

The subscript 2 on 11012 means the 1101 is written in base 2. Since numbers are normally written in base 10, a subscript 10 is usually only used when needed for clarity.

In base 10 notation, each place value represents a power of ten: the units place (100 = 1), the tens place (101 = 10), the hundreds place (102 = 100), the thousands place (103 = 1000), etc. So, for example:

9827   =   9 × 103  +  8 × 102  +  2 × 101  +  7 × 100

The subscript 2 in 100102 means the 10010 is in binary. The subscript 10 in 1810 means the 18 is in decimal.

Base 2 uses the same idea but with powers of two instead of powers of ten. Binary place values represent: the units place (20 = 1), the twos place (21 = 2), the fours place (22 = 4), the eights place (23 = 8), sixteens place (24 = 16), etc. So, for example:

100102   =   1 × 24  +  0 × 23  +  0 × 22  +  1 × 21  +  0 × 20   =   16  +  2   =   1810
