Lab 7: Dealing with Data Dos

This lab culminates students' introduction to functional programming with a data processing project using higher-order functions and two-dimensional lists. Students design a survey with multiple questions on a topic of their choosing. They then review item of and the higher-order function map and learn about ask and add as they create tools to collect data from other students, friends, or family. After collecting data, they review arithmetic operators and if then else and learn about all but first, length of list, and the higher-order functions keep and combine as they create various list processing tools to analyze their data and investigate their research topic.

As a reminder, functional programming is a programming paradigm in which computation is achieved by composing functions (reporter and predicate blocks) together rather than sequencing (i.e., ordering) command blocks. Instead of using iteration to process lists, it uses higher-order functions, which are functions that takes a function itself as input. Functional programming also treats data as immutable, so data cannot be changed once it is created. For example after their initial creation, lists cannot be modified with the add, insert, and delete command blocks.

This unit endeavors to prioritize functional programming as much as possible. This final lab uses imperative programming (uses commands to change the program's state) for gathering the data before switching to a strictly functional approach for data processing. We believe this is the first time a Middle School blocks-based programming course has taught functional programming first, and we seek to understand what works (i.e., engages, inspires, etc.) and what doesn't (i.e., confuses, frustrates, etc.).

Pacing

This lab is designed for 4–10 class periods (160–440 minutes).

  • Daily Activity: Computing in the News 5–15 minutes each day
  • Activity 1: Planning Your Survey: 25–50 minutes
  • Activity 2: Setting Up Your Survey: 10–20 minutes
  • Activity 3: Collecting Your Data: 30–60 minutes
  • Activity 4: Accessing Your Data: 10–20 minutes
  • Activity 5: Keeping Specific Data: 10–20 minutes
  • Activity 6: Combine-ing Data: 10–20 minutes
  • Activity 7: Creating Blocks for Combine: 15–30 minutes
  • Activity 8: Visualizing and Reflecting on Your Results: 30–140 minutes

Daily Activity: Computing in the News. 

Every day of BJC should begin with a 5-minute student presentation on a recent news article about computing. This helps build a foundation for considering the impacts of computing technologies on employment, issues of bias and accessibility, and tradeoffs between public access to information and security. Read Computing in the News for additional details and suggested news sources.

  • Prepare:
    • Assign to one student or a pair of students a recent news article about computing for them to read and then summarize for the class during the next class period.

Activity 1: Planning Your Survey. 

  • Materials: Planning Your Survey student page
  • Prepare:
    • Review the student page so that you understand the constraints on the student-selected research plans that will work well with this lab. Investigation topics should be complex enough to support two or more survey questions, and data processing strategies should rely on actual Snap! capabilities (described in the student page). Students should be encouraged to go beyond the options they are presented with, but these minimum constraints are designed to ensure that students are in a position to create a successful data processing project with this lab design.
    • Consider whether it will help your students to use this Planning Your Survey worksheet, and if so, prepare to share it with your class.
  • Learning Goals:
    • Design a survey with multiple questions to investigate a specific topic.
    • Create a data processing strategy to investigate the research question.
  • Activity Plan:
    • Designing a Research Plan: Students choose an investigation topic, and design a survey with two or more questions to research it. (10–20 minutes)
    • Designing a Data Processing Strategy: Students briefly consider the capabilities of Snap! that may be relevant to processing the data they collect, develop a rough plan for data collection (primarily to help ensure that their topic of investigation will yield a feasible project, and discuss their plan with a classmate. (15–30 minutes)
  • Tips:
    • You may prefer to discuss the constraints on students' research plans and co through the examples as a group before students begin working on the activity page to create plans of their own.
    • You may also opt to pause to discuss the capabilities of Snap! before students rough out their data processing strategy
    • You may wish to review students' research plans to check that they their plan will work well as a Snap! project with this lab.
  • Standards:
    • 2-DA-08: Collect data using computational tools and transform the data to make it more useful and reliable.
  • Solutions: This activity is creative and offline. Example investigation topics, survey questions, and strategies for investigating a question are included on the student activity page.

Activity 3: Collecting Your Data. 

  • Materials: Collecting Your Data student page
  • Prepare:
    • Before class, decide on the logistics for data collection. You may wish to have students leave their code open and rotate seats around the room or in small groups answering each other's questions. Or you may wish to have students remain in their seats, verbally collect data and enter it into their own surveys. In an online format, students may collect data in break-out rooms, or they may collect data from family and friends. You may wish to set a target number of people to collect data from based on the number of students and classroom dynamics.
  • Learning Goals:
    • Learn how to access a subset of values from a table.
    • Learn how to ask the user a question.
    • Review the higher-order function map, and use it to create a new survey response.
    • Learn how to add data to a table.
  • Activity Plan:
    • Asking the User a Question: Students review the structure of their table (a list of lists with only one list so far: their survey questions), learn how the questions only and ask blocks work, and discuss how to use these tools to populate their table of data. (10–20 minutes)
    • Adding New Responses to Your Table: Students use the map block to create a new response block that takes survey data as input, asks the user each question in the survey, and reports a list of the user's responses. They then use the add block to append a user response to the end of the my survey list, and test their code by adding a few responses to the survey. (10–20 minutes)
    • Collecting Data: Students remove their test data from the survey and then collect the actual data they will process to investigate their topic. (10–20 minutes)
  • Tips:
    • You may wish to pause to review the map block, which they learned about in Lab 4 Activity 4: Transforming Every Pixel, and/or to discuss how to use it to create the code for the new response block.

Activity 4: Accessing Your Data. 

  • Materials: Accessing Your Data student page
  • Prepare:
    • Work through this activity page on your own before your students.
  • Learning Goals:
    • Learn about and use the all but first block.
    • Review the higher-order function map, and use it to access a single column of data.
  • Activity Plan:
    • In the preliminary reading, students review the structure of the data in their table (using the image at right), see how the code for questions only uses the item of block, read about the intended functionality of the responses only block, and learn about the all but first. They then write and test code for the responses only block. (5–10 minutes)
    • Students then complete the code for the responses to question block to select a column of data by using map to apply item of to each row in the set of responses, and then they test their code. (5–10 minutes)
  • Tips:
    • Before moving on, you may wish to review students' code for responses only and responses to question.

Activity 8: Visualizing and Reflecting on Your Results. 

  • Materials:
    • Visualizing and Reflecting on Your Results student page
    • Recording Your Results worksheet
  • Prepare:
    • You may wish to review the process of exporting and importing blocks before your students work on this activity page.
    • Depending on time and student interest, consider whether you want some, none, or all of your students to import their data visualization blocks from their prior data project and use them to process this data.
    • Decide how you want students to respond to the reflection questions at the end (out loud to a partner, in the Google doc, on paper, etc.)
    • Consider whether you'd like to end the lab with students sharing their work, and if so, how you'd like to structure it. Sharing could be in the form of quick small-group shares or more formal presentations to the class.
  • Learning Goals:
    • Identify questions that can be answered using Snap! tools.
    • (Optionally) Import blocks from another project.
    • (Optionally) Use data visualization tools to create a pictograph of data.
    • Reflect on the research process.
  • Activity Plan:
    • Asking Additional Questions: Students create and answer additional questions about their data. (5–10 minutes)
    • Importing Blocks from Another Project: Students export blocks from their "Visualizing Data" project, import them into this project, and use them to create a pictograph of their data. (Optional: 10–20 minutes)
    • Reflecting on Your Results: Students consider what they have learned, who might benefit from the information, what else they could ask, what else they could build in Snap! to support research, and what other topics they'd like to research? (5–20 minutes)
    • Sharing: Allow time for students to share their investigation topic, survey questions, data, the way they processed their data, what they learned about their data, any visualizations they created, and their reflections. (20–90 minutes)
  • Tips:
    • Note that the sort data block requires counted data as input (that is, data that has gone through the count data block).
  • Standards:
    • 2-DA-08: Collect data using computational tools and transform the data to make it more useful and reliable.
  • Solutions: The results of this activity will vary based on students' questions and insights.

Correlation with CSTA Standards 

  • 2-DA-08: Collect data using computational tools and transform the data to make it more useful and reliable. As students continue to build on their ability to organize and present data visually to support a claim, they will need to understand when and how to transform data for this purpose. Students should transform data to remove errors, highlight or expose relationships, and/or make it easier for computers to process. The cleaning of data is an important transformation for ensuring consistent format and reducing noise and errors (e.g., removing irrelevant responses in a survey). An example of a transformation that highlights a relationship is representing males and females as percentages of a whole instead of as individual counts.
