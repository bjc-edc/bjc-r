Introduction to micro:bit

In this lab, you'll write a Snap! program in MicroBlocks to interact with a micro:bit.

Collect Materials

For each lab, we'll provide you with a list of materials to gather and some suggested materials.

  • micro:bit
  • USB cable
  • Battery pack (optional)

Set Up MicroBlocks

For this unit, you'll use a program called MicroBlocks, a Snap! programming language designed to control tools like micro:bit.

  1. Open MicroBlocks in either a Chrome, Firefox, Edge browser.
  2. Connect your micro:bit to your computer with a USB cable. Select your board from the browser dialog box, then click Connect.
  3. A green circle will automatically appear behind the USB icon, showing that the micro:bit is connected. If not, ask your teacher for help.

Your micro:bit is now connected, but you'll need to add more blocks to program it. You'll do this by adding a couple of libraries to MicroBlocks.

  1. Click the + symbol next to Libraries.
  2. Select Basic Sensors.ubl and then click open. Do the same for LED Display.ubl.

Make a Smiley Face

One block that's unique to micro:bit is the display block. It's designed to look like the grid of LEDs on one side of the board. There are 25 LEDs that you can turn on and off individually.

  1. Drag the display block into the Scripts area. Design your own face by clicking the LED rectangles to turn them on or off.
  2. Click on the block once and a smiley face will appear on your micro:bit.

Animate Your Face

Bring your face to life by adding a second display with another expression.

Tip: Animations are a rapid sequence of images displayed sequentially. To bring your face to life, you'll need to switch between display blocks quickly, but not too quickly. To achieve this, use a wait block after each display. Play with the amount of time until you find one that you like.

  1. Make a second expression using another display block.
  2. Create a loop to cycle through both expressions.

Make an Interactive Face

So far, your face changes on its own, but you can control when it changes between faces. One way to do this is to use the light sensor on the micro:bit. It can detect different levels of light, so you can change your micro:bit face whether its bright or dark (by covering the micro:bit with your hand).

Tip: Light levels vary greatly depending on the room that you're in, so you'll need to experiment with values for what's considered bright or dark. One way to do this is to use the say block. Click for a picture.
  1. Use an if statement and light levelto change the micro:bit's facial expression.

Take It Further

Use these ideas as a starting point to make your own animated faces. What other expressions can you try? How many "frames," or display blocks, can you incorporate to make a more detailed animation?

In this lab, you learned how to set up a coding environment for micro:bit, you used a display block to make create an image on a micro:bit using LEDs, and you created an animation using loops.
