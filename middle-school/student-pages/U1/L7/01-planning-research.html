Planning Your Survey

In this lab, you will design a more sophisticated research question on a topic that interests you, and you will create tools to gather data and analyze the results.

In this activity, you will choose a research question and design and set up your survey.

Designing Your Research Plan

In Lab 3: Dealing with Data, you created a survey with one question and collected data about one variable such as favorite ice cream flavor, music genre, fruit, sport, pizza topping, etc., and you created a pictograph of the results.

You've learned a lot since then, and you can now research a topic that will require multiple survey questions to investigate how two (or more) variables are related. And you can use Snap! to process the results for you.

For privacy, don't design a research plan that requires asking for personal information such as name, address, phone number, birthday, etc.

    • Possible Survey Questions:

    Example Research Question: Do people who have practiced music longer listen to more music?

    • Possible Variables: years spent playing music, hours spent listening to music each day
    • Possible Survey Questions:
      • How many years have you been playing music?
      • How many hours do you listen to music each day?

A variable is like a box that can store a value, such as a word, a number, or a list.

A table is a list of lists (a list with lists inside it).

This script:

  • Uses a variable called to store survey questions and responses
  • Checks whether my survey is already set up so it doesn't erase a survey you've already started
  • If my survey is not set up, sets up a table with the questions as the first row

How does work?


The is survey (my survey) not set up? block takes a list as input and reports true if either of the following are true:

  • The value stored in my survey is not a list
  • The value stored in my survey is an empty list

How does work?


The make survey with questions block takes a set of questions as input and reports a list containing one item: the list of questions. So, it sets up a table for the survey data as a list with one row containing the list of questions.

A watcher is a window that lets you see what value is stored in a variable.

On the Snap! stage, you will see your questions appear as the first row of a table in a watcher for the my survey variable. As you add responses to your survey, they will appear as additional rows in the table.

You can expand the "watcher" by clicking and dragging its lower right corner.

You can expand a column by clicking the space between the column headers at the top (right above the vertical line that separates the two columns).
